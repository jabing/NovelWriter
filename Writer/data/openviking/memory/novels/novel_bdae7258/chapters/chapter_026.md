**Chapter 26: The Price of Starlight**

The Starlight Archive crumbled at exactly midnight.

Not with thunder or flame. It simply… sighed.

Kael watched the last living grimoire dissolve into ash. The book had been singing an hour ago—a sweet, crystalline hum that filled the vaulted chamber. Now silence coated his tongue like grave-dust. He reached for the falling embers, but they passed through his fingers, dead and cold.

"No." His voice cracked. "Not yet."

He lunged for the preservation cases. The glass felt warm, feverish, trembling against his palms. Inside, a single phoenix feather pulsed with erratic light. The magic was guttering like a candle in a hurricane.

Kael pressed his forehead to the glass. "Hold on. Please."

The feather sparked once. Twice.

Then darkness.

The silence that followed was absolute. No more whispers from the enchanted shelves. No more ghost-lights dancing in the rafters. Just the heavy, oppressive quiet of a world forgetting its own enchantments.

Footsteps echoed behind him. Slow. Measured. Heavy with the weight of authority.

Headmaster Vane emerged from the shadows, his silver robes hanging loose on a frame that seemed to have withered in mere days. His eyes—once brilliant amber pools of prescience—were now the color of dishwater. Dull. Human. Dying.

"Twelve hours," Vane said. His voice rasped like dry leaves. "The eastern leylines dried at dusk. The western anchors will fail by noon tomorrow."

Kael's hands curled into fists. "The Archive was supposed to last another decade."

"Mathematics are for a world with constants." Vane approached the empty case. He touched the glass where the phoenix feather had been. "Magic is bleeding out faster than any prediction model allowed. The threads are unraveling, boy. All of them."

Kael stepped back. His boot crunched on the ashes of ten thousand years of accumulated wonder.

"Unless," Vane continued, turning his gaze on Kael, "we find a source. A spark sufficient to reignite the well."

"I don't know what you mean."

"Don't you?"

The Headmaster's hand shot out, faster than an old man should move. He seized Kael's wrist, wrenching it upward. Kael's sleeve fell back, revealing the scales.

They were beautiful and terrible. Opalescent dragon scales, embedded in a spiraling pattern from elbow to palm. They caught the moonlight—real moonlight now, cold and silver, where once the Academy had glowed with its own luminescence.

"You've been marked," Vane whispered. His grip tightened. "The dragon trusts you, Kael. Which means you know where it hides."

Kael jerked free. "Aurelion isn't a battery for you to drain."

" Aurelion is the last true font of raw magic in this realm!" Vane's shout cracked like a whip, sending dust cascading from the ceiling. "While we scrabble for crumbs, that creature hoards enough power to restore the leylines for centuries. The Dragon's Secret, Kael. The old texts are clear. One willing sacrifice. One heart of star-fire, freely given, and the magic returns."

"Freely given," Kael spat. "Or harvested by your butchers?"

Vane's expression softened into something worse than anger. Pity. "By noon tomorrow, there won't be enough magic left in the atmosphere to spark a candle. No healing for the sick. No wards against the things that wait in the dark. Do you understand? We are facing the end of civilization. Not in generations. Tomorrow."

He gestured at the ruin around them. "Is one beast worth more than every living soul?"

Kael looked at the ashes on the floor. He thought of Aurelion's laughter—yes, the dragon laughed, deep and rumbling like avalanches in spring. He thought of shared secrets in the high caves, of a friendship built on trust, not utility.

"I'll find another way," Kael said.

"There is no other way."

But Kael was already running.

The Forbidden Stacks breathed with stale air.

Kael moved through the darkness without a light—there was no magic left in his wand to ignite, anyway. His fingers traced the spines of forbidden tomes, volumes chained to the shelves with iron links now rusted and brittle.

*The Draconic Concordat*. He found it behind a false panel, exactly where Aurelion had described.

The book was heavy, bound in leather that felt warm and alive. Kael cracked it open on the reading pedestal, squinting at the archaic script. His heart hammered against his ribs.

*When the Well runs dry, only the Heart of the Last can refill it. The dragon must surrender that which makes it eternal: its flame, its memory, its name. Thus is the Secret, thus is the Price.*

Kael slammed the book shut. "No."

"So it's true."

He spun. Lysara stood in the aisle, her Inquisitor's blade drawn. The steel didn't glow with enchantment anymore. It was just metal. Sharp and mundane.

"You knew," she said. She looked tired. Her usually immaculate braid had come undone, strands framing her face. "All this time, you've been hiding the solution."

"It's not a solution, Lysara. It's murder."

"It's survival!" She stepped closer, blade steady. "The Council sent me to watch you. They knew you'd crack eventually. You wear your heart on your sleeve, Kael. You always have."

Kael backed against the pedestal. "You followed me?"

"You're predictable when you're frightened. You always run to the books." She gestured at the tome. "The dragon is in the Eastern Grotto. The old quartz caves. Don't look so surprised—dragons are creatures of habit, and that grotto resonates with their song."

"You can't tell them."

"I already have." Her eyes hardened. "I'm sorry, Kael. I really am. But I have a sister in the medical ward. The healing charms failed yesterday. She's bleeding internally, and the surgeons can't stop it without magic. By dawn, she'll be dead. Along with thousands of others."

Kael felt the world tilt. "You don't understand what you're doing. Aurelion isn't just… power. He's a person. He has dreams. He names the stars."

"And my sister has a name too." Lysara's voice broke. "Elena. She likes lavender and bad poetry. She’s seventeen. Please, Kael. Don't make me choose between her and your pet."

"He's not—"

"The Inquisitors are already moving. If you run now, you might reach the grotto first." She lowered her blade slightly. "But you won't stop them. The Headmaster has the Binding Chains. Forged in the First Age. Even a dragon can't break them."

Kael stared at her. At the desperation in her eyes. He saw the same look mirrored in Vane's face. The whole world, clawing at the edges of extinction, ready to tear apart anything that offered hope.

He moved.

Not toward her, but past her, shoving the pedestal over. Books crashed down in an avalanche of parchment and dust. Lysara cursed, stumbling back. Kael didn't wait. He sprinted down the aisle, boots slipping on the flagstones, and hit the service door at full speed.

The night air burned his lungs. The Academy grounds were wrong. The ever-blooming gardens were blackened sticks. The floating lanterns had fallen, shattered glass glittering on the paths like broken stars.

Kael ran faster than he ever had. The Eastern Grotto was three miles through the Whisperwood. Without magic, he couldn't teleport. Couldn't summon a steed. Just his own feet, his own heartbeat, his own terror.

Behind him, bells began to toll. Not the melodic chimes of study hours. The alarm. The hunt.

Branches whipped his face. His lungs seared. The path that had once glowed with fox-fire was pitch black, roots reaching to trip him. He fell once, twice, palms shredded on gravel. He didn't stop.

*Please*, he prayed to nothing. *Please wait for me.*

The grotto mouth loomed ahead, a jagged maw of quartz crystal. Once, it had shimmered with inner light. Now it was just stone. Dead stone.

Kael plunged inside. "Aurelion!"

His voice echoed. No answer.

Deeper. The tunnels narrowed. The air grew warm—impossibly warm. A good sign. The dragon's presence still heated the stone.

He burst into the cathedral cavern.

And stopped.

Aurelion lay coiled in the center of the grotto, silver scales dimmed to grey. The dragon was massive, a serpentine curve of muscle and crystal-bone that filled the space. But he looked small. Defeated.

One great eye opened, vertical pupil contracting. "You read the book," Aurelion rumbled. His voice was weaker than Kael had ever heard it. The usual resonance that shook stone was now a whisper.

"They're coming," Kael gasped, doubling over. "The Council. The Inquisitors. We have to move. Now."

"To where?" Aurelion lifted his head, wincing. Blood—bright, star-metal blood—dripped from his flank. Three long gashes, already festering. "The leylines are poisoned, Kael. I can feel them rotting. My magic is bleeding out with the world. Even if we ran, I couldn't fly. Couldn't fight."

Kael approached slowly, hands outstretched. "The Secret. You knew. You knew all along that you could fix this."

"I know my own heart, little human." Aurelion's laugh was wet, painful. "Yes. I could burn my essence. Refill the well. It would cost… everything. My memories. My self. I would become a machine of magic, mindless, eternal. A battery, as your Headmaster said."

"No." Kael reached the dragon's snout, placed his hands on the warm scales. "No, we find another way. There has to be another way."

"There isn't."

"There—"

"I have lived three thousand years," Aurelion said softly. His eye closed, a tear of molten silver tracking down his scales. "I have seen civilizations rise like flowers and fall like ash. But I have never had a friend. Not until you."

Kael's throat closed. He pressed his forehead against the dragon's snout. "Don't you dare. Don't you dare give up."
