Chapter 13: The Unraveling

The guardian spell unraveled into silver smoke the moment Kael's boot crossed the threshold.

He jerked back, but the damage was done. Ancient magical bindings that had protected the Academy's Restricted Archive for three centuries dissolved like sugar in rain. The runes carved into the marble archway flickered, gasped, and died.

"Kael!" Lyra's whisper was sharp enough to cut glass. She grabbed his collar, yanking him into the shadows between towering bookshelves. "You said you could disable the wards, not murder them."

"I did disable them." Kael stared at the dead archway. His hands trembled. "Temporarily. This was different. They just... stopped."

Lyra pressed her palm against the nearest shelf. Usually, the enchanted wood would hum against her skin, resonating with the ambient magic that saturated the Academy's foundations. Now, nothing. Just cold oak.

"It's accelerating," she breathed. Her eyes—normally the color of polished amber—looked almost bronze in the dim light. "The theoretical texts predicted a curve, not a cliff. The magic shouldn't be decaying this fast."

"Unless something's forcing it." Kael moved deeper into the archive, his boots silent on the dust-thick carpet. "We need that journal. If Malachar's research is here—"

"If we're caught here, expulsion will be the least of our worries." Lyra followed, her methodical nature warring with her curiosity. She clutched her satchel of detection charms. "Professor Thorne said the anti-intrusion wards would regenerate in twenty minutes. Without ambient magic, we might have five."

"Then stop talking and start searching."

They split between the aisles. The Restricted Archive smelled of ozone and dying lilies—scents that usually signaled powerful enchantments. Now, the air tasted flat. Metallic.

Kael ran his fingers along book spines. Titles floated in his vision: *On the Binding of Elementals*, *The Cartography of Ley Lines*, *Draconic Physiology: A Complete Study*.

There.

A slim volume bound in scales that shimmered even without magic: *The Aurelion Codex*.

Kael pulled. The book resisted, then shrieked as its preservation enchantment collapsed. Pages yellowed instantly, centuries of protection vanishing in a heartbeat.

"Got it," he called.

Lyra emerged from the next row, her face pale. "We need to leave. Now. I just checked my chronometer—the regeneration isn't happening. The wards aren't coming back. Something's eating the magic from the inside."

They hurried toward the exit. Kael cracked the codex open as they moved, scanning the dragon's elegant script. Aurelion had written this himself, centuries ago when the Academy was young.

"The bond must be willing," Kael read aloud, stumbling over an unfamiliar word. "The vessel cannot be forced, only given. To hold the spark is to become the—"

The lights went out.

Not just the magical lanterns. Everything. The emergency torches. The starlight filtering through high windows. Absolute darkness swallowed them, heavy and hungry.

"Kael?" Lyra's voice came from inches away, tight with fear.

"I'm here." He reached for her hand. Her fingers found his, ice-cold. "The exit's twelve steps ahead. Move slow."

They took three steps before the breathing started.

It wasn't human. It was the sound of wind through a canyon, of glaciers calving into frozen seas. It came from everywhere and nowhere, filling the darkness with the scent of ash and copper.

"Malachar's experiments," Lyra whispered. "The void husks. He said they were contained in the sub-basement."

"He lied."

The thing lunged.

Kael shoved Lyra aside and drew his knife—not steel, but obsidian, the only material that held an edge when magic failed. He slashed at the darkness. His blade connected with something that felt like frozen meat and screaming static.

Light erupted.

Not warm light. A sickly violet glare that illuminated the creature hovering before them. It had been a student once. Maybe. The uniform hung in tatters from translucent flesh. Where eyes should be, void spun like galaxies being born in reverse, sucking light into nothing.

The husk reached for Kael's chest.

"Run!" He drove the knife upward, feeling it scrape against ribs that weren't quite solid anymore.

Lyra didn't run. She grabbed a paperweight from a desk—a brass compass—and slammed it into the husk's skull. The creature staggered, hissing.

They bolted.

The archway loomed ahead, dead and dark. Beyond it, the Academy proper. Safety.

Ten steps. Five.

The husk shrieked. The sound hit Kael like a physical blow, stealing his breath, his balance. He tasted blood.

Then they were through, stumbling into the corridor. Kael slammed the iron door shut and turned the mechanical lock—no magic required, thank the old gods.

Silence.

"That wasn't supposed to be possible," Lyra gasped, leaning against the wall. Her hands shook as she pushed hair from her face. "Void husks need magic to manifest. If the fade is this advanced..."

"We need Aurelion." Kael clutched the codex to his chest. His heart hammered against the dragon's words. "Whatever's happening, he's at the center of it. He has to be."

The Dragon's Roost perched atop the Academy's eastern tower, a structure of crystal and obsidian that shouldn't have fit the Gothic architecture surrounding it. Aurelion had simply grown it there two hundred years ago, and the stone had learned to accommodate his presence.

Kael and Lyra climbed the spiral staircase, their footsteps echoing in the unnatural silence. Usually, the tower thrummed with residual power, the air thick with the dragon's ancient aura. Now, the steps felt like any other staircase. Dead stone.

The door at the top stood open.

Kael drew his knife again. Lyra produced a hand crossbow—mechanical, spring-loaded, the kind of tool that worked when spells didn't.

"Aurelion?" Kael called.

The great hall stretched before them, usually filled with mist and the dragon's shifting, iridescent form. Instead, they found emptiness. The pools of quicksilver had dried to gray dust. The bioluminescent gardens lay black and withered.

In the center of the desolation, Aurelion waited.

He was smaller than Kael remembered. Dragons weren't supposed to shrink. The creature coiled upon his dais, scales dull as river stones, wings folded tight against his ribs. When he lifted his head, his eyes—once molten gold—had faded to the color of winter sunsets.

"You brought the book," Aurelion said. His voice, usually a thunder that resonated in bone, came out as a rasp. "Good. There is so little time."

Kael approached slowly. "The magic isn't just fading. It's being hunted. We saw a husk in the Archive. It shouldn't have existed without magical substrate."

"Nothing should exist without magic," Aurelion agreed. He extended one claw, beckoning. "Come closer, children. Let me see what you have stolen."

Lyra held the codex while Kael opened it to the passage he'd found. The dragon's ancient gaze moved across the text—his own handwriting, centuries old.

"You read the words," Aurelion said, "but not the meaning. 'To hold the spark is to become the anchor.' Do you understand? I am not merely a dragon. I am the dam holding back the drought. And the dam is cracking."

"Malachar," Lyra said suddenly. Her eyes widened. "He's not trying to stop the fade. He's accelerating it. The experiments in the sub-basement—they're siphons."

The dragon's laughter was dry leaves skittering across stone. "Clever girl. Yes. Your professor believes that if magic dies completely, he can harvest the void that remains. A new power source. Cleaner. Controllable."

"He's insane," Kael said. "The void consumes. It doesn't create."

"Ambition makes its own madness." Aurelion shifted, and Kael heard the dry crack of failing joints. "I have held the line for eight centuries, but I am dying. When I fall, the fade will become a flood. Within days, all magic will vanish. Within weeks, the void husks will walk in daylight. Within months..."

He didn't finish.

"How do we stop it?" Kael stepped forward. "The codex mentions a bond. A willing vessel. Tell me."

Aurelion studied him. The ancient eyes held galaxies of sorrow. "You would give your life for magic you barely understand? You are seventeen, Kael Vane. You have not yet lived."
