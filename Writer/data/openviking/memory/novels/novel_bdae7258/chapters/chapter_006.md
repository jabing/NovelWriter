The crystal sphere shattered at exactly midnight.

Kael dove sideways as razored shards of enchanted glass sprayed across the Incantation Chamber. The luminescent fragments embedded themselves in the oak door behind him, hissing as they dissolved into toxic smoke.

"Containment's failing!" Master Virelle's voice cracked with panic she couldn't quite hide. Her hands wove frantic patterns in the air, silver light sputtering from her fingertips like a candle drowning in wax. "Kael, the secondary wards—now!"

Kael scrambled to his feet, shoving his own trembling hands into the ritual circle. The stone floor thrummed beneath his palms, but the vibration felt wrong—thin, reedy, like a harp string about to snap.

He shouted the binding words. The magic should have roared forth in a cascade of azure flame.

Instead, it wheezed.

A pathetic puff of sparks coughed from his palms and died. The remaining crystal fragments hovering in mid-air trembled, then plummeted to the ground with the dull clatter of mundane stone.

Silence swallowed the chamber.

Master Virelle lowered her hands slowly. Her eyes, normally sharp as daggers, looked hollow in the dim lantern light. She stared at the inert shards littering the floor—the same shards that should have been suspended in stasis for another century.

"Third failure this week," she whispered.

Kael wiped sweat from his forehead, his heart hammering against his ribs. "The fundamental enchantments are degrading faster than the Dean predicted."

"Faster than anyone predicted." Virelle kicked at the dead crystal pieces. They skittered across the floor like ordinary gravel, stripped of their magical luminescence. "The Academy's foundations were laid with permanent binding spells. If those are failing..."

She didn't finish. She didn't need to.

Kael bent to retrieve a shard, watching the last iridescent shimmer drain from its surface like color bleeding from a dying man's cheeks. Three hundred years of accumulated magical theory, and they were watching it turn to dust.

"What about Aurelion?" Kael asked quietly.

Virelle's head snapped up. Her expression shuttered instantly—that particular mask of neutrality every instructor wore when the dragon's name surfaced.

"Leave the restricted topics in the restricted stacks, Kael."

"With respect, Master, we just watched perpetual magic die in thirty seconds." Kael held up the dull crystal. "The texts say Aurelion predates the fading. They say he remembers when the world's veins ran full."

"Legends." But her voice lacked conviction. " bedtime stories to comfort first-years."

"Then why is the eastern wing guarded by sentinels who never sleep?"

Virelle crossed the chamber in three swift strides. She gripped Kael's shoulder, her fingers digging in with surprising strength.

"Listen carefully, boy. Curiosity about the Warden of the Deep Archives is the fastest way to find yourself expelled. Or worse—claimed."

She released him and turned away, her silhouette stooped against the darkened windows. Outside, the Academy's spires loomed like broken teeth against a starless sky. No magical lights danced along the parapets anymore. The enchanted lanterns had gone dark three nights ago.

"Clean this mess," she said softly. "Then go to bed. Forget what you saw."

But Kael was already memorizing the pattern of the failed spell—the particular way the magic had twisted before dying. It looked almost like something was drinking it.

Like something hungry.

***

The archives breathed around him.

Kael pressed his back against the cold marble pillar, counting the sentinel's footsteps. One-two-three-turn. The rhythm hadn't varied in forty minutes.

He'd traded his dormitory key for a cloaking charm from Mira, the alchemist's daughter. The charm tasted like copper and regret, and it made his skin itch as if ants crawled beneath his fingernails. But it worked. The sentinel—a construct of animated armor—stalked past his hiding spot without pause.

Kael waited three beats after the footsteps faded. Then he moved.

The Deep Archives lay beneath the Academy's foundation, accessible only through a staircase that appeared on no map. Kael had found it by following the magic.

Or rather, following the absence of it.

Everywhere else in the Academy, the air felt thin, stretched tight like over-washed linen. But here, in the eastern basement corridors, the pressure changed. The air grew heavy, dense with potential. It pressed against his eardrums and made his teeth ache.

He descended the spiral stairs two at a time, his lantern casting wild shadows against walls covered in forbidden glyphs. The symbols pulsed weakly, reacting to his presence.

At the bottom, a door waited.

It was not made of wood or iron. It appeared to be carved from solid midnight—the actual substance of night itself, compressed and shaped. Stars swam in its depths, dying and being born in endless cycles.

Kael reached for the handle. His fingers stopped an inch from the surface.

"You smell like desperation and stolen magic."

The voice didn't come from the door. It came from everywhere—the stone, the air, the shadows clinging to Kael's cloak.

Kael forced himself not to step back. "Aurelion?"

"Little moth, fluttering toward the flame." Something vast shifted in the darkness beyond the door. Scales scraped against stone, a sound like mountains colliding. "Do you know what happens to moths?"

"They burn," Kael said. His voice sounded braver than he felt. "But they also illuminate what the dark tries to hide."

A pause. Then—a laugh like rolling thunder, warm and terrible.

The door swung open without being touched.

Beyond lay a cavern that shouldn't exist beneath the Academy. The ceiling soared into darkness, lost in shadows that moved with slow, tidal grace. And coiled within that darkness, filling the space with the scent of ozone and ancient storms, was the dragon.

Aurelion's scales gleamed like burnished gold dulled by centuries of tarnish. Each plate was the size of Kael's torso, etched with circuits of light that pulsed in arrhythmic patterns. His eyes—vertical pupils set in molten amber—fixed on Kael with the weight of geological time.

He was smaller than the histories claimed. Only the size of a cathedral, then.

"You seek answers about the fading," Aurelion rumbled. Smoke vented from his nostrils, carrying sparks of dying embers. "Everyone does, eventually. When their spells falter. When their children are born with hollow eyes, unable to touch the weave."

Kael stepped across the threshold. The air here tasted electric, rich with the memory of what magic had once been.

"You know why it's happening," Kael said. It wasn't a question.

"I know why." Aurelion lowered his massive head until one dinner-plate eye levelled with Kael's face. The vertical pupil contracted, examining him with surgical precision. "The question is whether you can pay the price to stop it."

Kael's mouth went dry. "What price?"

"The same price that was paid to ignite the world's magic originally." Aurelion's tail swept across the cavern floor, disturbing centuries of dust. Where it touched, the stone bloomed with temporary luminescence—flowers of light that wilted instantly. "A sacrifice of source. A giving-back."

"I don't understand."

"No. You don't." The dragon's voice dropped to a whisper that still shook Kael's bones. "Your kind takes. Magic flows upward— from the deep places, through the dragons, to the surface walkers. You drink and drink, never questioning the well."

Kael thought of the failing spells, the fading crystals, the empty lanterns. "The well is running dry."

"Because we are dying." Aurelion extended one claw, obsidian black and sharper than any blade. He touched Kael's chest, lightly, over the heart. "My brothers and sisters have faded one by one over the millennium. I am the last. When I go, the conduit closes."

Kael looked up into the ancient, burning eyes. "Then teach me how to save you."

"You cannot save me, child. But you can replace me."

The claw pressed harder, not breaking skin but pressing with the promise of penetration. Kael felt his heartbeat accelerate, hammering against the implacable digit.

"The ritual requires a vessel," Aurelion hissed. "A human heart to replace the draconic one. A mortal soul to fuel the eternal flame. You would live forever, burning from within, feeding the world your life-force drop by drop."

Kael's blood turned to ice. "I'd become the source?"

"You would become the sacrifice. Immortal, yes. Conscious, certainly. But in agony eternal, as the world drinks your essence to power their parlor tricks." Aurelion withdrew his claw. "That is the secret your Masters fear to know. That is why they guard this door. Not to protect me from you. To protect you from the choice."

Kael stumbled backward, his mind reeling. The weight of it crushed him—the implication that every spell ever cast, every enchanted sword and flying carriage, had been fuelled by the slow consumption of dragons.

And now, the last dragon offered him the throne of martyrdom.

"I need time," Kael choked out.

"You have until dawn." Aurelion settled his head upon his forelegs, closing his eyes. "When the sun touches the Academy's highest tower, I will fade entirely. The Age of Magic ends with me. Unless..."

He didn't finish. The implication hung in the charged air like the pause between lightning and thunder.

Kael turned and ran. He sprinted up the spiral stairs, past the sleeping sentinels, through the corridors that suddenly felt too narrow, too fragile. His lungs burned. His mind raced faster than his feet.

He burst into the Academy's main hall just as the first grey light of dawn began to stain the eastern windows.

And stopped.

Master Virelle stood in the center of the hall, surrounded by the entire faculty. Their faces were grim, resigned. In her hands, Virelle held a ceremonial dagger of black glass—the Kindling Blade, used only in executions and dark bindings.

"Kael," she said softly. Her eyes were red-rimmed, wet with tears she refused to shed. "The dragon spoke to you."

It wasn't a question.

Kael looked at the blade, then at the professors blocking every exit. He understood suddenly. They knew. They had always known. And they had been waiting for someone desperate enough, foolish enough, to open the door.

"You're not going to let me choose," Kael realized.

"We cannot let the magic die," Virelle said. She stepped forward, the blade catching the dawn light. "I'm sorry. But we need a volunteer."

The doors slammed shut behind him, enchanted locks clicking into place with the finality of a coffin lid.
