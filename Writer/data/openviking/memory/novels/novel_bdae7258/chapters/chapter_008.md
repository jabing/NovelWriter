"The spell shattered in Kael's hands like spun glass hitting stone."

Luminescent fragments dissolved into gray smoke before hitting the floor. The acrid scent of ozone and dying embers flooded the cramped archive chamber. Kael stumbled backward, clutching his singed fingers as the last motes of amber light flickered and died.

"Not again," he whispered.

The preservation glyph had held for three centuries. Now it couldn't survive five minutes in his grasp. Kael stared at his palms, watching the residual mana drain away like water through a sieve. The Dying wasn't just accelerating—it was *hungry*.

He kicked the empty ritual circle in frustration. Iron scraped against granite, the sound shockingly loud in the midnight silence of the Academy's Restricted Stacks. If the Watchwardens heard him, he'd face expulsion. Or worse, the Silence Chambers.

But he had to know.

Kael dropped to his knees beside the scattered remains of the broken spell. His fingers traced the etchings on the floor, following the fractured lines to where they pointed—always pointing—toward the sealed eastern wall. Every failed enchantment in the Academy seemed to lean that direction lately, like flowers bending toward a hidden sun.

"What's down there?" he muttered.

The wall looked solid granite, veined with quartz that caught the moonlight filtering through high windows. But when Kael pressed his ear against the stone, he heard it. A rhythm. Not machinery. Not water. Something vast and breathing.

Thrum. Thrum. Thrum.

The heartbeat shook dust from the rafters.

Kael yanked his head back, heart hammering against his ribs. The sound wasn't coming from beyond the wall.

It was coming from *inside* it.

"Step away from the barrier, boy."

Kael spun. Archivist Vex loomed in the doorway, her silver robes swallowing the dim light. Her eyes weren't the milky blue of sleep—they blazed with alert, dangerous awareness. In her right hand, she held a staff that shouldn't exist anymore: true oak, living wood, humming with stored power that had become rarer than diamonds.

"You shouldn't be here," Vex said. Her voice carried the weight of thirty years enforcing Academy doctrine. "The West Stacks are forbidden after moonrise."

"I was studying," Kael lied, positioning himself to block the wall. "Advanced Transmutation theory."

Vex's staff tapped the floor. The sound resonated through Kael's teeth. "Transmutation doesn't scream when it fails. That was a Seventh-Tier binding breaking. I felt it three floors up."

She advanced, moving with the fluid grace of someone who had trained in martial magic before the Dying made such combat unsustainable. Kael retreated until his shoulder blades hit the suspicious wall. The thrum behind him intensified, matching his pulse.

"What's behind here?" Kael demanded. "Why does every spell I cast point toward this wall?"

Vex stopped. For a moment, the hardness in her face cracked. Kael saw something terrifying there—not anger, but grief.

"You've noticed the alignment," she said quietly.

"Everyone's noticed! Magic is running downhill like rainwater. But nobody will say where it's draining." Kael pressed his palm flat against the stone. The wall was warm. "It's here, isn't it? The Aurelion?"

Vex's staff wavered. "Where did you hear that name?"

"The preservation glyph I tried to save—it wasn't a spell at all. It was a lock." Kael's mind raced, connecting fragments from six months of secret research. "The Dying isn't natural. Someone is *taking* the magic. Storing it."

"Enough." Vex raised her staff. Light—not the pale ghost-light of modern casting, but true golden radiance—gathered at its tip. "You will forget what you've seen. The Academy demands it."

"I won't be silenced while the world goes dark!"

Kael didn't think. He acted.

He reached into his coat and pulled out the one spell he'd managed to save from the Dying—a shard of crystallized mana, black-market purchased, worth more than his life. He crushed it in his fist.

Power exploded through his arm.

It felt like grabbing lightning. Kael screamed as the raw magic surged through pathways never meant to channel such force, but he aimed it—not at Vex, but at the floor beneath her feet.

The granite rippled like water.

Vex leaped aside with a curse, her concentration shattered. The compulsion spell on her staff died uncast. Kael didn't wait. He spun and slammed both palms against the warm wall, feeding the last dregs of the crushed crystal into the stone.

"Open, damn you!"

The wall recognized the magic. It had been waiting for it.

Stone dissolved like mist. Kael fell forward into darkness, tumbling through a threshold that shouldn't exist in three dimensions. He hit a spiral staircase winding down into impossible depths, rolling and skidding until he slammed against a bronze railing.

Above, Vex's voice echoed: "Kael, don't! You don't understand what he requires!"

He scrambled up, bleeding from his forehead, drunk on residual mana. The staircase descended into a cavern that predated the Academy, predated the city, perhaps predated humanity itself. Bioluminescent fungi painted the walls in shades of cyan and violet, revealing the scale of what lay below.

The chamber was a throat. A gullet. And at its bottom, something coiled.

Kael descended, each step carved from obsidian that drank the light. The air grew thick, pressurized, rich with the scent of storms and ancient incense. His skin prickled—not with fear, but with potential. Here, magic wasn't dying. It was sleeping.

"You're smaller than the last one," rumbled a voice that wasn't sound but pressure against his bones.

Kael reached the final step.

The cavern opened into a cathedral of stone ribs and suspended bridges long collapsed. In the center, chains—not metal, but condensed starlight and frozen time—wrapped around a form that hurt to perceive directly.

Aurelion.

The dragon filled the space like a mountain fills a valley. His scales were midnight blue, each one inscribed with runes that shifted and rewrote themselves constantly. But his eyes—golden pupils slit against molten copper—held Kael pinned more effectively than any chain.

"Another thief," Aurelion said. His voice created small cyclones in the dust. "Come to take what little remains?"

"I'm not a thief." Kael's voice cracked, but he stood straighter. "I'm trying to save the magic. To stop the Dying."

Aurelion's laugh was thunder trapped in a bottle. The chains blazed white-hot, and the dragon winced, smoke rising from his wings where the bindings cut.

"Save it?" Aurelion lowered his massive head until Kael could see his own reflection in one dinner-plate eye. "Child, I *am* the Dying. Every spell cast in this world for three centuries has drawn from my blood. The Academy built their tower atop my prison and called it progress."

Kael's blood ran cold. "You're the source?"

"I am the reservoir. The battery. The wound that never closes." Aurelion's tail twitched, shattering a stalactite with the resulting shockwave. "They siphon my essence to power their lights and locks. When I am empty, the magic ends. As designed."

"Designed by who?"

"The founders. Your precious Archmages." Aurelion's gaze flicked toward the ceiling. "They feared free magic. Too chaotic. Too democratic. So they caged the last true dragon and turned the world into a leech."

Kael thought of the failing spells, the students weeping as their incantations turned to ash, the way light-globes dimmed earlier each year. "If you're the source, you can restore it. You can reverse the Dying."

"I could." Aurelion's chains groaned as he shifted, revealing his underbelly—scarred, wounded, weeping light that evaporated before hitting the ground. "If I were free. But freedom requires a Key. And the Key requires a price your kind has never been willing to pay."

"What price?" Kael stepped closer, ignoring the heat radiating from the dragon's form. "I'll pay it. Whatever it is."

Aurelion studied him for a long moment. The bioluminescence pulsed in rhythm with the dragon's heart.

"Your predecessors said the same. The last candidate stood where you stand eighty years ago. He chose to maintain the prison. To let me suffer slowly rather than risk the alternative." Aurelion's voice dropped to a whisper that filled Kael's skull. "The Key is not an object, boy. It is a vessel. A replacement. To free me, someone must take my place in these chains. Must become the new source. Immortal. Imprisoned. Alone."

The weight of the words settled over Kael like a shroud.

Above, footsteps thundered down the staircase—Vex and the Watchwardens, coming to drag him back to the light. To safety. To the slow, comfortable death of a world consuming its own magic.

Aurelion extended one claw, its tip sharp as broken promises. "Choose. Take my place, and magic returns to the world. Walk away, and the Dying continues until I am dust, and your children's children grow up in a world of iron and silence."

The footsteps grew louder. Shouts echoed.

Kael looked at the claw. He looked at the chains.

Then he reached out and grasped the dragon's talon.

"Show me," Kael said, "how the chains fit."

Aurelion's eyes widened. For the first time in centuries, the ancient dragon smiled.

And the world began to change.
