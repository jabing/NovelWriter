The spell shattered like glass, and the sky began to bleed.

Kael Vane threw himself sideways as the weave collapsed. Raw, screaming magic tore through the air where his head had been. It struck the ancient stone wall behind him, not burning or blasting, but *unmaking* it. Gray dust puffed into nothing. The mortar forgot how to bind.

"Kael!" Lyra Ashford's voice cut through the howl of dying enchantments.

She was fifty feet back, sheltered behind a bronze bulwark she'd erected. Her hands moved in precise, jerking patterns, sketching equations in the air with luminescent chalk. Not casting. *Measuring*.

"It's accelerating!" she shouted. "The decay rate just doubled!"

Kael didn't need her mathematics to feel it. The air tasted like copper and forgetting. His own magic—the warm, familiar pulse in his chest—felt slippery, as if his grasp on it was loosening finger by finger.

He raised his hands and pushed back against the unraveling. Golden light sparked from his palms, weaving a net of stabilization. The threads caught, held for three heartbeats, then snapped.

The backlash drove him to his knees.

Pain lanced through his skull. Nosebleed. Classic symptom of arcane rejection. The magic was fighting him, or rather, the lack of it was. Like trying to swim in evaporating water.

"Get out of there!" Lyra screamed.

Too late. The Fracture widened.

A jagged line of *nothing* split the earth between them. Not darkness. Absence. The grass didn't burn; it simply ceased to have color, then form, then existence. The Fracture hungered toward Kael's boots.

He lunged backward, grabbing for the rope they'd anchored to solid rock. His fingers closed on hemp. He hauled, muscles burning.

The world tilted.

For one sickening moment, gravity stuttered—the magical constant that held things down failing. Kael floated, weightless, as the Conservatory ruins around him turned monochrome. Stone became gray mush. Steel rusted to powder in seconds.

Then the rope snapped taut. Lyra had braced herself against the bulwark, heels dug in, pulling with desperate strength. Kael crashed back into normal air, gasping.

The Fracture sealed with a sound like a sigh.

Silence returned to the valley, heavy and ominous.

"That was closer," Kael wheezed, wiping blood from his chin.

Lyra didn't answer. She was staring at her chalk equations, which now hung frozen in the air, slowly rotating. Her eyes were wide behind her spectacles.

"Lyra?"

"It's not random." Her voice trembled with the particular fear of a scholar who has just disproven their safest theory. "The decay vectors. They're all pointing the same direction."

She flicked her wrist. The equations rearranged, aligning into an arrow that pointed northeast. Toward the Obsidian Spire.

Toward Malachar.

---

The campfire didn't warm them.

It wasn't for lack of wood or flame. The fire burned bright enough, orange and crackling, but the heat seemed to stop three inches from Kael's skin. Dissipating into the thinning weave of reality.

"The Siphon Heart." Aurelion's voice rumbled from the shadows above.

The dragon descended from the cloud cover. Or rather, he fell.

Kael's heart clenched. Three weeks ago, Aurelion had been magnificent—scales like molten gold, wings that eclipsed the sun. Now he crash-landed fifty yards from camp, his landing sending tremors through the ground. His scales were dull bronze, flaking. One wing dragged at an awkward angle.

He was shrinking.

"You're getting smaller," Kael said, rising to meet him.

"I am being *consumed*." Aurelion's great eyes, once amber lanterns, were now dimmed to candlelight. "As is all magic. Every spell cast, every enchanted blade drawn, every hearth-fire lit with a whisper... it flows to him now."

Lyra stepped forward, clutching her notes. "Malachar found it, then. The First Relic."

"He found more than that." Aurelion lowered his massive head to their level. His breath smelled of ozone and old books. "He has become the Relic. The Siphon Heart beats in his chest. He is no longer merely a wizard, nor even a warlock. He is a *wound* in the world."

Kael's hands curled into fists. "Then we cut it out."

"If only steel could suffice." Aurelion's chuckle became a wet cough. Sparkles of dying magic drifted from his maw like glittering ash. "To destroy the Siphon, one must sever the weave at its source. A Final Weaving. A... closing of the wound."

"Teach me," Kael said.

"No."

The dragon's refusal struck like a physical blow.

"Why not?" Lyra demanded. "If it's the only way—"

"Because the conduit dies." Aurelion's gaze fixed on Kael with terrible gentleness. "The spell requires a vessel to channel all the stolen magic back into theThreads of Creation. The influx would burn any mortal soul to cinders. It is not sacrifice, children. It is annihilation."

The wind howled across the barren plateau. Somewhere in the distance, another Fracture opened—a sound like tearing silk.

"I'll do it," Kael said.

"Absolutely not." Lyra stepped between him and the dragon, her back straight, her composure cracking only in the tightness around her eyes. "We find another way. We always find another way."

"There is no other way." Aurelion's wings drooped. "I have lived three thousand years. I have seen the rise and fall of the Arcane Kingdoms. I have watched the Threads fray for centuries, but this... this is theft. This is murder. If Malachar is not stopped within the turning of the moon, the last magic will vanish. Forever."

"Then I'll be the conduit," Kael said quietly.

Lyra spun on him. "You don't get to decide that alone!"

"Someone has to!" He grabbed her shoulders, forcing her to meet his eyes. "Lyra, look at him. Look at the world. It's *dying*. If my life buys back the magic for everyone else—"

"For how long?" Her voice broke. "Another generation? Two? Then someone else has to die? This isn't a solution, Kael. It's a stay of execution!"

"She speaks wisdom," Aurelion murmured. "But wisdom will not stop the Spire."

They stood in stalemate. The fire popped and hissed, a sound too mundane for the apocalypse they discussed.

Then the temperature dropped twenty degrees.

Frost spiderwebbed across the ground, not from cold, but from the sudden, violent absence of heat magic. The fire extinguished with a whimper.

"He's here," Aurelion growled.

Malachar didn't walk into their camp. He *unfolded* into it.

One moment, the plateau was empty. The next, reality bent like a curtain pulled aside, and the antagonist stepped through. He looked different than Kael remembered. Taller. His skin glowed with a sickly, internal luminescence—veins of liquid silver visible beneath translucent flesh. His eyes were voids, absolute black, drinking in the starlight.

Behind him, the air screamed softly.

"Kael Vane," Malachar said. His voice harmonized in three octaves, resonating with stolen power. "And the lovely Lyra. And... oh. The lizard."

Aurelion roared, but the sound was weak. Pathetic.

Malachar smiled. "Don't strain yourself, old worm. I have what I need from your kind already."

Kael stepped forward, putting himself between Malachar and Lyra. His hands itched to cast, but he dared not. Every spell he threw would only feed the monster before him.

"Come to gloat?" Kael asked.

"Come to offer mercy." Malachar spread his hands. Silver light dripped from his fingertips, eating holes in the stone where it landed. "Join me. Both of you."

Lyra laughed, bitter and sharp. "You want us to help you destroy the world?"

"I want you to help me save it." Malachar's void-eyes fixed on Kael with terrible intimacy. "You feel it, don't you? The slipping. The fear that your next spell will be your last. I can stop that. The Siphon doesn't destroy magic—it *concentrates* it. In me. I can hold the weave together. I can be the Anchor."

"At the cost of everyone else," Kael spat.

"At the cost of chaos." Malachar's voice softened, became almost fatherly. "Your parents understood, Kael. They tried to help me complete the Siphon, twenty years ago. They were the first donors to the cause."

The world went silent. Even the wind stopped.

"What?" The word tore from Kael's throat.

"They didn't die in the Fracture at Thornhaven." Malachar tilted his head, curious. "They were sacrifices. Necessary ones. Their bloodline—your bloodline—was bred specifically to stabilize volatile weaves. You're not just a wizard, boy. You're a *battery*. And I need to recharge."

Kael didn't think. He couldn't.

He reached into the fading well of his power and *pulled*.

Golden light exploded from him—not the controlled weaves of a trained caster, but raw, ancestral fury. The plateau shook. The sky above them split with aurora light.

Malachar's smile widened. "Yes. Good. Feed me."

The Siphon activated.

Kael felt his magic tear away, not gradually, but in a torrent. It felt like having his veins ripped open. He screamed, falling to his knees, as his essence flowed into Malachar's outstretched hands.

Lyra shouted his name.

She lunged—not at Malachar, but at Kael. She tackled him, breaking the line of force. They rolled across the frozen ground.

"Lyra, don't—" Kael gasped.

But she wasn't looking at him. She was looking at Aurelion. At the dragon's eyes. Some silent communication passed between them.

"Do it," she whispered.

Aurelion opened his jaws. Not to breathe fire—to sing.

The Draconic Chord. The First Song. The magic-before-magic.

The sound resonated in Kael's bones, in the iron in his blood, in the very gravity that held him down. It was a frequency that predated the Siphon. Predated Malachar.

It was a door.

"Lyra, what are you—" Kael started.

She kissed him. Hard, desperate, brief.

Then she shoved him backward into the sonic gateway Aurelion had torn in the world.

"No!" Kael reached for her.

Their fingers brushed.

Then the weave swallowed him, and he fell through screaming space, away from the plateau, away from the dying fire, away from Lyra standing to face Malachar alone with nothing but her empty hands and her courage.

The last thing he saw before the void took him was Malachar's smile faltering for the first time.

And Lyra beginning to burn with silver light.
