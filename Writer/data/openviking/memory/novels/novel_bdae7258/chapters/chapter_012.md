Chapter 12: The Last Reflection

The scrying mirror cracked right down the middle, silver veins spiderwebbing across the glass.

Kael lunged forward, hands burning with the last dregs of his lumina, trying to hold the image together. Aurelion's golden eye stared back at him from the reflection—ancient, knowing, fading.

"Hold on," Kael gasped. Sweat dripped from his chin onto the obsidian frame. The mirror was three centuries old, one of twelve artifacts still capable of piercing the Veil between the Academy and the dragon's prison. Now it was dying like everything else.

The lumina in his palms flared amber, then guttered to sickly yellow. Wrong. Three months ago, a sustained channeling spell would have blazed white-hot for hours. Today, Kael had twenty minutes before his magic ran dry. Twenty minutes to learn why the world's last dragon had chosen *him*.

"You waste your light, little flame," Aurelion rumbled. The voice didn't come from the mirror—it resonated in Kael's sternum, vibrating his teeth. "The tether snaps regardless."

"Then tell me fast." Kael gritted his teeth, feeding another thread of lumina into the crack. Pain lanced up his wrists. The magic felt like pushing honey through a sieve, thick and reluctant. "How do I stop the Fade?"

The golden eye blinked. Slowly. When Aurelion spoke again, the words carried the weight of mountains falling. "You cannot stop what they have started. You must steal back what they have taken."

"Who? The Dean? The Council?" Kael leaned closer. The mirror's surface rippled like disturbed water. "What did they take?"

Aurelion's scales shifted in the reflection, copper and bronze catching light that shouldn't exist in the dragon's lightless cell. The image flickered. Kael poured more power into the connection, feeling the familiar hollowness open in his chest—the sensation of his very life force draining into the spell.

"The First Spark," the dragon whispered. "They hide it beneath your feet, Kael. Beneath the—"

The mirror exploded.

Not outward. Inward. The glass imploded with a sound like a thousand wineglasses shattering in reverse, sucking the air from the Vault of Whispers. Kael threw himself backward, crashing into a shelf of grimoires. The lumina in his hands died instantly, leaving him blind in the sudden dark.

Silence reigned.

Kael lay gasping on the cold marble floor, his fingers numb, his magic reduced to a whisper in the back of his skull. The Fade had never hit so hard, so fast. He fumbled for the ever-torch on his belt, clicking the ignition crystal three times before a pathetic orange flame sputtered to life.

Where the mirror had stood, only a black scorch mark remained on the pedestal. The air smelled of ozone and burnt cinnamon—the scent of broken enchantments.

"Impressive," said a voice from the doorway. "Most students merely *read* the forbidden texts. You prefer to destroy them."

Kael's blood turned to ice.

Archmage Vaelin stood silhouetted against the Vault's entrance, his silver robes catching the torchlight. The Dean of Practical Magics wasn't supposed to patrol the lower archives until after midnight. It was barely tenth bell.

"I was researching stabilization techniques," Kael said, climbing to his feet. His legs shook. "For my thesis on—"

"On draconic resonance?" Vaelin stepped into the room. His eyes—pale blue and currently glowing with the telltale shimmer of truth-seeing—fixed on Kael's face. "Don't lie to me, boy. I heard the conversation. The entire east wing heard Aurelion's voice."

Kael's hand drifted toward his empty belt. His focus crystal was in his dorm room, standard Academy policy preventing students from carrying amplified power sources in restricted areas. He was defenseless.

"He's lying," Kael said. "The dragon. He tells stories to anyone who listens."

"Does he?" Vaelin circled the scorched pedestal, trailing fingers through the ash. "Then you won't mind explaining why you've visited this vault seven times this month. Or why your lumina signature matches the disturbances in the Foundation Level."

The Dean looked up, and his expression wasn't angry. It was pitying. That was worse.

"You feel it, don't you?" Vaelin asked softly. "The hollowness. The way spells require more and yield less. You think Aurelion offers salvation from the Fade. You think the dragon holds the key to restoring what we've lost."

Kael said nothing. His heart hammered against his ribs.

Vaelin sighed. He reached into his robes and withdrew something that made Kael's breath catch—a vial of liquid starlight, pure condensed lumina, swirling with impossible brilliance. It should have been impossible to contain. Magic that concentrated should have dissipated immediately in the current age.

Unless...

"We are not villains, Kael," Vaelin said. "We are surgeons cutting out rot. The First Spark cannot be returned to the world. Not if civilization is to survive."

Kael's eyes locked on the vial. "You're hoarding it."

"We are *concentrating* it." Vaelin stoppered the vial away. "Aurelion's prison isn't a cage, boy. It's a reservoir. The dragon is the dam holding back the flood. If he breaks free, if the Spark returns to the wild magic of the world..."

The Dean's face hardened. "Every spell you've ever cast will unravel. Every enchantment holding this Academy together will invert. We are talking about cataclysm."

"You're talking about control," Kael said. His voice surprised him—steady, sharp. "The Fade isn't natural. You're drinking the ocean and blaming the tide."

Vaelin's hand twitched. For a moment, something dangerous flickered in his eyes.

"Detention becomes imprisonment," the Dean said quietly. "Imprisonment becomes execution. You have one chance, Kael. Tell me what Aurelion promised you. Tell me where the dragon thinks the Spark is hidden."

Kael thought of the dragon's final words. *Beneath your feet.*

The Foundation Level. The restricted sub-basements where students never went.

"He didn't say," Kael lied.

Vaelin studied him for a long moment. Then he reached out and touched Kael's forehead.

The world went white.

Pain—pure, electrical pain—shot through Kael's skull. Not a spell. Something older. Something that reached into his thoughts and *rifled*, turning memories inside out like pockets. He screamed, falling to his knees, seeing his own thoughts projected in the air—Aurelion's eye, the cracked mirror, the scent of cinnamon.

"Interesting," Vaelin murmured, rifling through the visions. "He showed you the Seal. He thinks you can break it."

"Stop," Kael choked out. He tried to summon lumina, but his reserves were dust. The Fade had taken everything.

The pain intensified. Kael's vision tunneled. He saw the Foundation Level now, not as memory but as invasive knowledge forced into his mind—vast chambers of machinery, pipes drinking magic from the earth, the First Spark trapped in a prison of spun diamond and regret.

He saw the truth.

Aurelion wasn't the prisoner. The dragon was the lock.

And someone was turning the key.

"Enough," Vaelin said, withdrawing his hand. Kael collapsed, gasping, saliva dripping from his chin. "You see now. You understand why we cannot allow—"

The floor shook.

Not a tremor. A heartbeat. Deep, rhythmic, shaking dust from the ceiling in rhythmic pulses. Boom. Boom. Boom.

Vaelin stumbled, catching himself on the pedestal. His eyes went wide. "No. He can't. The bindings—"

Kael felt it then. In the empty place where his magic had been, something answered. Not lumina. Something hotter. Something that didn't care about the Fade or the First Spark or the rules of magic.

Aurelion's presence filled the room, not through the broken mirror, but through the cracks in the world itself.

The dragon spoke, and this time, every student in the Academy heard it. Every sleeping mind woke screaming. Every spell in active casting inverted, exploding in showers of impossible color.

"The cage breaks," Aurelion said, and his voice was everywhere and nowhere, shaking the Foundation walls. "The thief who drinks the ocean will drown, little mages. Kael—run."

The floor beneath Kael's feet cracked open, revealing the machinery below.

And from the depths, the First Spark looked back.
