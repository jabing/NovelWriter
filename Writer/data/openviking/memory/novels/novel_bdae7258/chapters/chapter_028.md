The crystal vault cracked at midnight, and the last recorded dragon-song spilled into silence.

Kael lunged across the reading table, fingers scrabbling for the floating resonance stone. It dropped through his grasp like smoke, hitting the marble floor with a sound like breaking bone. The blue light guttered. Died.

"No," Kael whispered. 

The archival chamber shuddered. Books leaped from shelves as the levitation enchantments failed. Heavy tomes crashed down, centuries of accumulated spellcraft slamming into dust.

Professor Thorne grabbed Kael's collar, hauling him backward. "Move, boy! The structural wards are collapsing!"

They stumbled through falling debris. Kael clutched his satchel, feeling the weight of the stolen key inside—the one he'd lifted from the Headmaster's office three hours ago. Guilt and terror warred in his chest.

A crack split the ceiling. Not stone breaking. Reality itself.

Through the fissure, Kael saw stars where there should have been solid rock. The night sky bled through, impossibly distant and cold. The dying magic was eating the boundaries between places.

"The Archive is unraveling," Thorne shouted over the groan of grinding stone. "This is what happens when the ley lines run dry!"

"But we knew this would happen," Kael said, dodging a falling chandelier. Its enchanted flames had turned to guttering yellow, no longer the eternal blue-white of preserved magic. "We have months. Years, maybe."

"Not anymore." Thorne's eyes were wild, bloodshot. "Someone's accelerating the decay. Someone's *drinking* the remaining power."

They burst through the iron doors into the corridor. Behind them, the Archive imploded with a sound like a thousand windows breaking. The concussion threw Kael against the opposite wall. His teeth clicked together, tasting copper.

Silence fell, heavy and absolute.

Kael pushed himself up, staring at the doorway. Where the vault had been, there was now only a gray void. No rubble. No dust. Just absence, as if the room had never existed.

"Three centuries of magical history," Thorne said, his voice hollow. "Gone."

"Not all of it." Kael reached into his satchel. His fingers closed around cool crystal—the memory shard he'd managed to extract before the collapse. It pulsed weakly against his palm, holding the last thirty seconds of the dragon-song recording. "I saved Aurelion's voice."

Thorne spun, his robes whipping. "You what?"

"The resonance frequency. It's not just a recording, Professor. It's a map."

Kael held out the shard. Within its facets, a golden thread spun, weaving patterns that matched no known constellation. But Kael recognized the shape. He'd seen it in the forbidden texts he'd been sneaking from the restricted stacks for weeks.

"It's pointing under the Academy," Kael said. "Aurelion isn't dead. He's here. Beneath us."

Thorne went pale. The old professor's hands trembled as he reached for the shard, then jerked back as if burned.

"You're talking about the Hollow," Thorne said. "No student goes there. No *living* thing goes there and returns sane."

"The magic is dying," Kael said, stepping closer. "We need the secret he holds. The key to restoration. We don't have time for caution."

"The dragon is chained for a reason, Kael. The founders—"

"The founders were wrong!" Kael's voice cracked, echoing down the empty corridor. "They locked him away and built this school on top of his prison to siphon his power. That's why the Academy has lasted while the rest of the world's magic fades. We've been parasites."

Thorne stared at him. The silence stretched, broken only by the distant sound of alarms beginning to ring—wardens responding to the Archive's destruction.

"If we go down there," Thorne said quietly, "we don't come back up without answering to the Headmaster. To the Council. They'll know we violated the First Compact."

"Then they can join us," Kael said. He turned toward the service stairs, the ones that descended into the bedrock. "Because I'm going down. Tonight."

Thorne hesitated. The alarms grew louder, red light beginning to pulse along the corridor walls.

"Damn you, boy," Thorne muttered. "Wait. I'm coming."

***

The descent took an hour that felt like a year.

The stairs spiraled down through strata of limestone and something older—black stone that drank the light from their torches. Kael's breathing sounded too loud in the narrow passage. Behind him, Thorne muttered wardings under his breath, but the protective spells fell flat, dissolving into the stagnant air.

"Feel that?" Thorne asked.

Kael nodded. Pressure building behind his eyes. A hum in his teeth, resonating from the crystal shard in his pocket. The deeper they went, the stronger it grew.

Not a hum. A heartbeat.

"The ley lines aren't dead down here," Kael realized. "They're concentrated. Twisted."

"They're *corrupted*," Thorne corrected. "The founders didn't just imprison Aurelion. They turned his cage into a reservoir. Every spell cast at the Academy for three centuries has been filtered through his essence."

"Draining him."

"Destroying him."

They reached the bottom. An iron door stood before them, its surface covered in chains that moved with slow, serpentine undulation. Not metal. Serpents of shadow and old magic, guarding the threshold.

Kael reached for the door.

"Don't," Thorne warned. "The guardians will—"

Kael pressed the memory shard against the iron. The golden thread within flared, singing a single pure note.

The serpents recoiled. They hissed in harmonies that made Kael's blood freeze, then slithered aside, coiling into the corners. The door groaned open, revealing a cavern that breathed.

"Impossible," Thorne breathed. "The guardians should have destroyed any intruder."

"They recognize their master's voice," Kael said. "Aurelion wants us to enter."

The chamber beyond was vast, larger than the Academy above. Stalactites of crystallized magic hung from the ceiling, pulsing with dying light. And in the center, held by chains thicker than oak trees, was the dragon.

Aurelion was not what Kael expected.

He wasn't scales and wings and fire. He was *infrastructure*—a being of woven light and crystallized starlight, his form shifting between physical and ethereal. Chains of pure binding logic pierced his essence, anchoring him to the stone. Where they entered his body, the light turned septic, green-black with corruption.

He had no eyes, only voids that contained galaxies. They opened, turning toward Kael.

"Little thief," Aurelion said. His voice was the sound of mountains grinding together, of forests burning, of hope crystallizing into desperate prayer. "You stole my voice. Now you bring it home."

Kael forced himself to step forward. The air tasted of ozone and ancient sorrow.

"We need the secret," Kael said. His voice barely carried across the space. "The world is dying. The magic is fading. You know how to restore it."

Aurelion laughed. The sound shook dust from the ceiling. The chains rattled, spitting sparks of wasted power.

"I know," the dragon rumbled. "I am the last Source. The wellspring from which your ancestors drew. They bound me here to fuel their convenience, and now the well runs dry."

"Then tell us how to refill it," Thorne said, stepping beside Kael. His scholarly awe battled with terror on his face. "Tell us the restoration ritual."

"There is no ritual," Aurelion said. His void-eyes fixed on Kael, seeing too much. "Only payment. Magic is neither created nor destroyed, little mortals. It is exchanged. Your world fades because the debt comes due."

Kael felt cold certainty settle in his stomach. "What kind of payment?"

"Life for life," Aurelion whispered. "Power for power. To restore what has been spent, a Source must burn. I am the last. If I die, the magic returns—briefly, brilliantly, a final sunset before eternal night."

"No," Thorne said. "There must be another way. A cycle, a renewal—"

"There is one alternative," Aurelion said. His form shifted, chains clanking. "A replacement. A new Source to take my place. To be chained here, forever, feeding the world with their essence until they, too, are consumed."

The dragon's gaze locked on Kael.

"You have the affinity, thief. You resonated with my song. You could hold the power. You could save your world."

"Don't listen," Thorne grabbed Kael's arm. "It's a trap. A trick to swap places—"

"Is it?" Aurelion asked. "The Archive fell tonight. Tomorrow, the Wards. Next week, the healing spells that keep your sick children alive. How many will die while you search for solutions that do not exist?"

Kael looked at his hands. He'd spent his life studying magic, desperate to feel it fully before it vanished entirely. Now he stood at the precipice of having all the power in the world, at the cost of everything he was.

"I need time," Kael said.

"You have minutes," Aurelion said, his head lifting. "They come."

From above, muffled by stone but growing louder: boots. Many boots. The Headmaster's guard.

"They've tracked us," Thorne said. "Kael, we must—"

"Choose," Aurelion commanded. "Take my place, and I will teach you to restore the flow before I fade. Or flee, and watch the darkness claim everything you love."

The dragon extended one massive claw, its tip hovering inches from Kael's chest. Within the crystalline talon, Kael saw visions: the Academy restored, magic blazing golden across the world, children laughing as spells worked properly again.

And beneath those visions, the price. Lonely centuries in the dark. The slow erosion of self. Madness. Death, eventual and inevitable.

"Kael!" Thorne shouted. "The door!"

Iron crashed above. Shouts echoed down the stairwell.

Kael reached out, his hand trembling, hovering over the dragon's claw. One touch would seal the bargain. One touch would save the world.

Or destroy himself.

The first mage-guard burst through the doorway, lightning crackling at his fingertips, screaming Kael's name as a traitor.

And Aurelion whispered: "Choose."
