**Chapter 22: The Silence Between Stars**

The spell shattered three feet above the abyss.

Kael felt the weaves unravel in his hands—threads of luminescent force snapping like rotted spider silk. One moment he was gliding across the chasm on a bridge of solidified starlight; the next, he was falling.

*Not yet,* he thought. *Not like this.*

"Kael!" Lyra's voice cut through the howling wind.

He twisted mid-air, fingers scrabbling for purchase against the cliff face. Stone tore at his palms. His boots scraped against rock, finding nothing. The death-wind of the Hollow Expanse clawed at his cloak, hungry for the warmth of living things.

Below, the darkness wasn't empty. It was full of the Absence—the anti-magic that had been devouring the world for three months.

Kael reached for his core, that inner furnace where mana should burn bright. He found only embers. The Fade was accelerating.

"Grab the rope!" Lyra shouted.

A silver line whipped past his face. He seized it with bleeding fingers. The jolt nearly dislocated his shoulder, but he held on. Above, Lyra had anchored herself to an outcropping, her boots dug into ancient stone, both hands wrapped around the enchanted cord.

It was fraying.

Even the rope—woven by the Weaver Monks with magic meant to outlast empires—was turning to dust in her grip.

"Climb!" she screamed. "Climb now!"

Kael hauled himself up, hand over hand, muscles burning. The rope dissolved thread by thread under his weight. He didn't look down. He looked at her—at Lyra's fierce green eyes, her dark hair whipping in the gale, the determination that had carried them across three dying kingdoms.

Ten feet. Five. Two.

The rope snapped.

Kael lunged. His fingers closed around her wrist. Lyra grunted, sliding forward, her cheek scraping stone. For a heartbeat, they hung there—two human weights balanced on the edge of oblivion.

Then she heaved backward, using the last of her failing kinetic magic to pull them both onto the ledge.

They collapsed together, gasping. Kael's heart hammered against his ribs like a trapped bird. Lyra's hand found his, her fingers ice-cold.

"Did you feel it?" she whispered. "At the end?"

Kael nodded, throat dry. "No resistance. The magic didn't fight back. It just... stopped."

"Like a candle running out of wax."

They lay there, listening to the silence where the wind had been. Even the air felt thinner now, less real. The Expanse had been a treacherous crossing for centuries, but now it was becoming impossible. Without magic, the floating pathways between the Spires would vanish. The barriers holding back the Wildnight would fall.

The world was running out of time.

"We're close," Kael said, sitting up. His hands shook. He hid them in his sleeves. "Aurelion's lair is through that arch."

He pointed to a passage carved into the cliff face—an entrance framed by petrified dragons' teeth, each one taller than a man. Beyond lay the Sanctum of Ash and Memory, where the last true dragon waited.

If he was still alive.

"If he's dead," Lyra said, reading his thoughts, "we have no map to the Source. No way to stop Malachar."

"Then we'd better hope dragons don't fade as fast as human sorcery."

They helped each other stand. Lyra checked her satchel—inside, the Detection Compass spun wildly, its needle hunting for magical north but finding only chaos. She snapped the case shut.

"Lead the way," she said.

But she didn't let go of his hand.

The tunnel breathed.

Kael had expected darkness, but the Sanctum glowed with bioluminescent fungi—pale blue mushrooms that pulsed like a dying heartbeat. With every step deeper into the mountain, the light grew dimmer. The Fade was here too, eating even the natural magics of the deep earth.

"Aurelion!" Kael called. His voice echoed off walls carved with epics in High Draconic. "We came! Just as the prophecy—"

"Prophecies are residue," a voice rumbled from the dark. "Echoes of magic that once meant something."

The chamber opened like a flower of stone. And there, coiled upon a bed of melted gold and forgotten crowns, lay Aurelion.

Kael's breath caught.

The dragon was smaller than the stories claimed. Not the mountain-sized terror that had burned the Legion of the Sun, nor the winged city from the bard's tales. This Aurelion was the size of a carriage, his scales the color of rusted copper, his wings folded tight against his sides like a bat's.

But it was his eyes that held them. Ancient. Yellow. Fading.

The iris of his left eye was turning gray.

"You're young," Aurelion said. His voice was gravel and distant thunder. "And your magic is thin. The boy has perhaps three fireballs left in him. The girl... something else. Something reservoir-shaped."

Lyra stepped forward, chin high despite her fear. "The Fade. It's accelerating. We need to know how to stop it. The Grand Library said you were there when the First Spell was woven. You know the Source."

"I am the Source," Aurelion whispered. "Or was. Once, dragons were the conduit between the world's soul and its skin. We breathed magic into the atmosphere. Every spell ever cast by human hands was borrowed from our dreams."

He shifted. The sound was like heavy chains dragging across stone.

"But someone is draining the dream," the dragon continued. "Siphoning it. Not just taking magic, but unmaking the possibility of magic. You feel it as emptiness. I feel it as death."

"Malachar," Kael said. The name tasted like ash.

Aurelion's good eye narrowed. "The Sorcerer-King. Yes. He found the Heart beneath the Obsidian City. He thinks to become the new Source. To replace us. To be the only wielder of power in a world of mundanity."

"We can stop him," Kael said. "Tell us how to reach the Heart. We'll destroy it, sever the connection—"

"No!"

The word boomed with enough force to crack the stone beneath their feet. Dust rained from the ceiling. Aurelion reared up, and for a moment, the ancient power was visible—a ghost of the titan he had been.

"Destroy the Heart," the dragon hissed, "and magic dies forever. Not just fades. Dies. The Heart is not a vessel to be emptied. It is a door. And Malachar is wedging it open to drain the other side."

Lyra's hand went to her mouth. "He's killing the world to fuel his own immortality."

"He's killing the world because he fears death," Aurelion corrected. "But he is a fool. The door swings both ways. If he drains the ethereal plane completely, the vacuum will consume the material world. Silence. Absolute zero. Not even dust remains."

Kael felt cold certainty settle in his stomach. "Then we don't destroy the Heart. We seal it. Cut him off."

"How?" Lyra asked. "If he's already bonded to it—"

"Sacrifice," Aurelion said softly. The dragon lowered his great head until they were eye-level. His breath smelled of ozone and old libraries. "Someone must take his place. Willingly bond with the Heart, become the new living Source. A replacement battery, if you will. Human souls were never meant to hold such weight. The candidate would burn. Brightly, briefly, and then... ash."

The chamber fell silent.

Kael looked at Lyra. She was already looking at him.

"No," they said simultaneously.

"You don't get to decide for me," Lyra said, turning to him. Her eyes were bright with anger. "I'm the methodical one, remember? I calculate odds. You rush in. This requires precision, control—"

"It requires someone willing to die," Kael interrupted. He took her shoulders. "Lyra, if you bond with that thing, you won't just die. You'll be scattered across the weave of reality. No afterlife. No memory. Just... fuel."

"And if you do it?" she snapped. "You're the warrior! The one with 'destiny' written in his bloodline! What happens to the rebellion without its symbol?"

"I'm not letting you—"

"This isn't about letting!" Her voice broke. "It's about mathematics! Who can hold the weave longest? Who can give the world time to find a permanent solution?"

Aurelion watched them, his expression unreadable. "The girl is correct. Her magical signature is... unusual. Dense. She would last perhaps a decade as Source. You, boy, would last three years. Maybe four."

Kael felt the words like a physical blow. A decade. Lyra could give the world ten years to solve the Fade permanently. He could give it three.

"I won't allow it," he said.

"You can't stop me," she replied quietly.

"Actually," Aurelion rumbled, "he can. And so can I."

The dragon moved faster than something so large should move. His tail whipped around, not striking them, but striking the wall. A hidden mechanism groaned. Stone grated against stone.

The exit sealed.

"The Heart chooses," Aurelion said. "Not the willing, but the worthy. And Malachar... he is not the only one who prepared for this day."

The dragon's gray eye—the fading one—suddenly blazed with silver light.

Lyra gasped. She clutched her chest, doubling over. Kael reached for her, but an invisible force threw him back. He hit the wall hard, stars bursting in his vision.

"Lyra!" he choked.

She was glowing.

Not the warm light of hearth-magic or the sharp brightness of combat sorcery. This was cold, stellar radiance—the light of the space between stars. It poured from her eyes, her mouth, the veins in her wrists.

"The blood remembers," Aurelion intoned. "The Ashford line. Descendants of the First Weavers. You are not just a candidate, child. You are the lock *and* the key."

"No," Kael whispered. He struggled to his feet, pushing against the pressure that held him pinned. "Aurelion, stop! There's another way!"

"There is," the dragon agreed. "But the Sorcerer-King arrives within the hour. He has tracked you here. He knows what she is."

The light around Lyra intensified. She floated three inches off the ground, hair streaming upward as if underwater. When she looked at Kael, her eyes were entirely silver—no whites, no pupils.
