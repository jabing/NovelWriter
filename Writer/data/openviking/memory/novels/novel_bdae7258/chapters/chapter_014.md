The light spell died in Kael's palm.

Not flickered. Not faded. *Died.*

One moment the mana burned bright and blue between his fingers; the next, it unraveled into silver smoke that tasted of ozone and forgotten winters.

"Kael!" Lyra's voice cut through the hum of the Arcane Lab. "The containment!"

He spun. The glass prison across the chamber—usually shimmering with seven layers of binding enchantments—had gone dull as pond water. Inside, a whisper-wraith pressed against the cracking crystal, its form shifting from smoke to something with too many teeth.

The wraith smiled.

"Get down!" Kael lunged, tackling Lyra behind an alchemy station as the containment sphere exploded.

Shards of enchanted glass rained down like hail, each fragment screaming as the magic bound within them hemorrhaged into the air. The wraith didn't drift out. It *poured*, a black tide of hunger that absorbed the light from the room.

Kael scrambled for his focus crystal, but the stone was cold. Dead. The ambient mana in the Academy's laboratory—the thickest concentration of magical energy in the Western Provinces—had gone quiet as a grave.

"This isn't possible," Lyra whispered, her breath hot against his ear. "The wards here are reinforced with dragonbone. They should last centuries."

"Should," Kael agreed, drawing his dagger. Steel wouldn't hurt a wraith, but it felt better than empty hands. "But they're not."

The wraith coiled toward them, leaving frost-rimed cracks in the stone floor. It was feeding. Not on flesh, Kael realized with dawning horror. On the residual magic in the walls. In the air. In *them*.

"Run," Kael said.

"Where? The doors are sealed by the failsafe protocols."

"Then we unseal them." Kael grabbed her hand, pulling her toward the emergency lever. "The old way. No magic. Just physics."

They sprinted. The wraith screamed—a sound like tearing silk—and gave chase. Kael felt the cold snap at his heels, felt the strange suction as the creature tried to drink the life-force from his blood. His heart hammered against his ribs, each beat a defiant drum in the silence where magic should be.

Lyra slammed into the lever. The iron mechanism groaned, resisted, then surrendered with a shriek of rusted metal. The blast doors rumbled open.

They stumbled into the corridor as the wraith hit the threshold—and recoiled. Silver light still flickered in the hallway runes, weak but present. The creature hissed, retreating into the darkened lab.

"Seal it," Kael gasped.

Lyra already had her hands on the emergency plate, her fingers dancing across the mechanical lock. No spells. Just sweat and desperation. The doors thundered shut, trapping the wraith inside.

Silence returned, heavier than before.

Kael pressed his forehead against the cooling metal. "That's the third time this week."

"Third time magic has failed mid-cast, or third time something has tried to eat us?" Lyra's voice trembled, but her hands were steady as she checked him for injuries.

"Both."

They stood in the flickering corridor of the Academy's lower levels, surrounded by walls that should have glowed with perpetual light. Instead, shadows pooled in corners where none had existed yesterday. The great Tapestry of Magical Theory—woven by the First Archmages—hung opposite them, its golden threads graying before their eyes.

"It's accelerating," Lyra said, touching the fraying fabric. "The Dissolution. We thought we had decades. Maybe years. But look at this."

She pointed to a thread representing Elemental Binding. It was turning to dust even as they watched.

"We need to see the Provost," Kael said.

"Malachar?" Lyra's eyes narrowed. "He'll blame us for the lab. Say we disturbed the enchantments."

"He's the only one with access to the sealed archives. If there's a record of the last time magic faded—"

"The Dragon's Age," Lyra finished. "When Aurelion supposedly stopped the collapse."

"Aurelion stopped it," a voice purred from the shadows, "because Aurelion started it."

They spun. Malachar stepped from the gloom of a side passage, his robes immaculate, his smile sharp enough to cut glass. In his hand, he held a lantern—but instead of flame, it contained a swirling void that seemed to drink the light around it.

"You children play with forces you cannot comprehend." Malachar's gaze drifted to the sealed lab doors. "Releasing wraiths. Disrupting the Weave. Tell me, did you enjoy watching your spells turn to ash?"

"We didn't release anything," Kael said, stepping between Lyra and the Provost. "The containment failed because the magic binding it disappeared."

"Disappeared?" Malachar's laugh was soft, dangerous. "Or was it *taken*?"

He raised the lantern. The void within pulsed. In the lab behind the sealed doors, the wraith shrieked again—but this time in agony. Kael heard the sound of rushing wind, of suction, of something being *dragged* against its will.

Then silence.

Malachar lowered the lantern. A new thread of silver light swirled within the void, captive and struggling.

"You see," he said, "magic isn't dying, young Vane. It's being *harvested*. And you two have just led me to the largest remaining reservoir of wild mana in the Academy."

Lyra's hand found Kael's, her fingers ice-cold. "The wraith was bait."

"Clever girl." Malachar's smile widened. "Though not clever enough. The creature was drawn to your little light spell—the last true working of the Old Weave in this building. Everything else here is mine now. Every enchantment. Every ward. Every spell your fellow students try to cast tomorrow morning will simply... stop."

Kael's mind raced. The failing lights. The graying tapestry. The way his crystal had gone dead in his hand. "You're draining the Academy."

"I'm draining the *world*, boy. One nexus at a time." Malachar opened his palm, and the air between them distorted. "But I need one more thing to complete the collection. A spark from the old bloodline. A Vane has never tasted true emptiness, has he? Your family always kept the dragon's secrets close."

Kael reached for his dagger, but the weapon felt wrong—heavy, inert. The steel was enchanted, he remembered too late. Malachar's influence had turned it to dead metal.

"Run," Kael told Lyra.

"No," she said, surprising him. She stepped forward, chin raised, her empty hands raised in a casting stance. "You want old magic, Provost? Have it."

She spoke a word that hurt to hear. It wasn't from any language Kael recognized—not Elvish, not Draconic, not the High Tongue of the Archmagi. It was older. Deeper.

And it worked.

Light exploded from her palms—not the blue-white of standard luminescence, but golden fire that smelled of summer and ancient things. The corridor blazed. Malachar stumbled back, his void-lantern shattering against the wall.

"What—" he snarled.

Lyra grabbed Kael's arm. "The roof. Now."

They ran. Behind them, Malachar's scream of rage shook the stones. Whatever Lyra had done, it wouldn't hold him long.

They burst through a service stairway, climbing past dormitories where students slept unaware that the enchantments protecting their dreams had been stolen. Past the Great Hall where the Founder’s Statue wept stone tears as its animating magic bled away.

On the roof, the night air bit sharp and cold. Stars wheeled overhead, indifferent to mortal struggles. Kael gasped, doubling over, his lungs burning.

"How did you do that?" he panted. "That spell—it wasn't in any curriculum."

Lyra leaned against the parapet, her face pale as moonlight. "Because it isn't magic. Not as we know it. It's... memory. Inherited. My grandmother called it the Dragon's Echo."

"Aurelion taught your grandmother?"

"Protected her. During the last Dissolution." Lyra turned to face him, her eyes reflecting starlight. "Kael, Malachar isn't just stealing magic to hoard it. He's trying to kill the dragon. Aurelion is the anchor. The Weave is tied to his life-force. If Malachar drains the dragon completely—"

"Magic dies forever," Kael finished. "Not fades. Dies."

A shadow passed over the moon. Great wings blocked the stars, silent as death. Kael looked up to find eyes like burning sapphires staring down at them—ancient, vast, and terribly wounded.

Aurelion landed on the Academy's highest spire, and the stone groaned beneath his weight. The dragon's scales, usually gleaming like molten gold, were dull and cracked. Where his heart should have beat with luminous power, there was only a flickering ember.

"You spoke my name," the dragon rumbled, his voice like distant thunder. "Few mortals remember it now."

"We need your help," Kael called out. "Malachar is—"

"I know what Malachar is, Kael Vane." Aurelion lowered his massive head, smoke trickling from his nostrils. "I know because I created him."

The world seemed to tilt.

"What?" Lyra whispered.

"Three centuries ago, when the first Dissolution threatened to unmake reality, I needed a guardian. A reaper of wild magic to prevent the chaos from consuming everything." Aurelion's eyes closed in what might have been shame. "I gave him the lantern. I taught him the hunger. I did not foresee that he would develop... ambition."

Kael's hands clenched into fists. "You made a monster."

"I made a tool. The monster grew later." Aurelion extended one claw, and Kael saw that the tip was bleeding light—pure, liquid mana dripping into the darkness. "He has grown too strong. The lantern I gave him can hold the magic of a thousand wizards. Soon, it will hold mine. And when it does, he will become something worse than a man without magic. He will become the void itself."

"How do we stop him?" Kael demanded.

The dragon's gaze locked onto Kael, piercing and endless. "You cannot. But you can replace me."

Lyra sucked in a breath. "The binding. The old stories. A mortal can take the dragon's burden."

"Not any mortal," Aurelion said. "A Vane. Your bloodline was forged in the first fires of the Weave, Kael. You can become the new anchor. You can hold the magic of the world within your soul, keep it safe from Malachar's hunger... but you will burn. Slowly. Painfully. And when you finally extinguish, magic dies with you, unless another takes your place."

Kael looked at his hands. The hands that couldn't even hold a light spell tonight. The hands of a boy who had failed every advanced casting examination.

"How long would I last?" he asked quietly.

"Decades," Aurelion said. "Perhaps a century, if you are strong. But you would be alone. Isolated. The magic would sustain you, but it would also consume you."

"Kael," Lyra said, her voice breaking. "No."

"Is there another way?" Kael asked.

"Yes," the dragon admitted. "You could kill Malachar. Destroy the lantern. Release all the stolen magic back into the Weave in one catastrophic surge. It would restore the world..."

"But?"

"But the release would require a sacrifice. A death. And Malachar has bound the lantern to his life-force. To destroy it, you must destroy him. And to reach him..." Aurelion looked toward the stairway.

The door burst open. Malachar stepped onto the roof, his robes billowing in the sudden wind. In his hand, the void-lantern burned darker than before, full of stolen starlight and dying spells. His eyes had gone completely black, pits of absolute nothing.

"To reach him," Malachar said, his voice layered with terrible harmonics, "you must first get past me. And I am very, very hungry."

He raised the lantern. The roof tiles beneath Kael's feet began to crumble as the magic holding them together was ripped away.

Aurelion roared, a sound that shook the city, but the dragon was too weak. The golden fire that had answered Lyra's call flickered and died as the lantern turned its hunger toward the dragon's heart.

Kael stood at the edge of the spire, caught between the void and the dying light, with only seconds to choose.

Become the anchor and burn alone forever.

Or kill Malachar and die in the attempt.

"Choose quickly," Malachar whispered, and the darkness reached for Kael's soul.
