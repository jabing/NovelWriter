The ward shattered like glass made of starlight.

Kael threw himself sideways as the protective barrier dissolved into silver dust. The dust didn't fall—it evaporated, leaving nothing but the sour taste of ozone and the screaming silence of dead magic.

Behind him, Lyra's gasp cut through the hush.

"It's accelerating," she whispered, clutching her grimoire to her chest. Her knuckles matched the bone-white pallor of the pages. "The decay hit reinforced enchantments in seconds. Yesterday it took hours."

Three figures in obsidian masks stepped through the dissolving archway. Their blades didn't gleam. Instead, they absorbed the dim lantern light, hungry voids in the shape of swords.

"Malachar's Hollowed," Kael said, reaching for his own faltering magic.

His fingers sparked amber, then guttered into smoke. The familiar warmth of his power flickered like a candle in a hurricane, there and gone before he could shape it.

"He knows we deciphered the map," Kael said, backing toward the vault's only other exit. A dead end. "He knows we found Aurelion's resting place."

The first assassin lunged.

Kael rolled, grabbing a fallen candelabra. Iron and wax wouldn't stop void-steel, but physics still worked when magic failed. He smashed the heavy base into the Hollowed's mask.

Ceramic cracked. Black ichor sprayed.

"Run!" Kael grabbed Lyra's hand.

They sprinted through the collapsing corridor as the Academy's ancient foundations groaned. Stone that had been held together by thousand-year-old binding spells began to crumble. The air itself felt thin, as if reality were developing leaks.

Lyra skidded to a halt at the balcony overlooking the East Wing. Her eyes widened.

"Kael. Look."

Below, in the great hall where three hundred students had gathered for morning assembly, spells were failing in cascading waves. A student levitating for a demonstration plummeted six feet. Healers rushed to help as protective circles around the younger children flickered and died.

The color was literally draining from the tapestries. Threads unraveled into gray ash.

"It's not just the Academy," Lyra said, her voice trembling despite her usual composure. "It's the Weave itself. The fundamental fabric."

"Then we don't have time for stairs," Kael said.

He climbed onto the balustrade, ignoring her protest. Three stories down. Normally, he'd soften the landing with a kinetic buffer. Now?

Now he jumped.

The wind tore at his coat. For three terrifying seconds, he was weightless, magicless, utterly vulnerable.

Then Lyra's hand closed around his wrist.

Her spell—woven with mathematical precision, anchored in pure logic rather than raw force—caught them both three feet from the floor. They landed hard, knees buckling, but alive.

"I thought you said your evocation was failing," Kael panted.

"Calculus doesn't fade," Lyra said, pulling him upright. Her eyes were fever-bright. "Only magic. Come on. If Malachar's here, he isn't after us. He's after the dragon."

They ran.

***

The catacombs beneath the Academy had once glowed with phosphorescent fungi and guardian runes. Now they were dark, the fungi reduced to black slime, the runes cracked and empty.

Kael held his lantern high. The flame barely pushed back the shadows.

"According to the Star-Chart of Eldrin," Lyra whispered, consulting her notes by flickering light, "Aurelion sealed himself beneath the Founder's Crypt when the Dying began. He knew. He's always known why magic is leaving the world."

"And he never told anyone," Kael said, bitterness creeping in. "Just let us watch it happen. Watch children lose their gifts. Watch the Healers' hands go cold."

"Maybe he couldn't tell. Maybe the secret itself is dangerous."

"Everything is dangerous now."

They reached the sealed door. Or rather, where the door had been. Now there was only a hole, the edges melted as if by dragonfire.

Kael drew his knife—not for the assassins, but for whatever lay beyond.

The chamber beyond was vast, carved from obsidian and crystal. And in the center, coiled around a pillar of pure, radiant amber, was Aurelion.

The dragon was smaller than the legends suggested, no bigger than a house cat when coiled. His scales shimmered with trapped starlight, and his eyes—ancient, depthless pools of gold—opened as they entered.

"Finally," Aurelion said. His voice wasn't loud, but it resonated in Kael's bones. "The entropy has reached the Academy. I had hoped for another century."

"Tell us how to stop it," Kael demanded, stepping forward. "People are dying. Magic is the only thing holding this world together, and it's bleeding out. Tell us the secret."

Aurelion uncoiled, stretching wings that seemed to fold into dimensions Kael couldn't perceive. The dragon's gaze fixed on Kael, then Lyra, weighing them.

"There is no stopping it," Aurelion said softly. "Only replacing it."

Lyra stepped forward, methodical even in the face of a deity. "Replacing it with what? Another source? A different Weave?"

"With a sacrifice," Aurelion said. "The magic isn't dying, children. It's migrating. Returning to the Source. And for it to remain in this world, someone must become the Source. A vessel. A living anchor."

Kael's blood ran cold. "Malachar. He wants to be the vessel."

"He intends to be," Aurelion corrected. "But he misunderstands the nature of the burden. The vessel doesn't control the magic. The magic controls the vessel. He would become a tyrant of force, yes, but a puppet of power. The world would survive, but burn."

"Then we have to stop him," Kael said. "We have to find another way. Someone else could—"

"Could what?" A new voice cut through the chamber, smooth as silk and sharp as a scalpel. "Volunteer?"

Malachar stepped from the shadows near the dragon's pillar. He wore academic robes, impeccably clean despite the chaos above. His smile was gentle, his eyes were not.

"Young Kael," Malachar said, spreading his hands. "So predictable. So eager to rush toward martyrdom. But I'm afraid the position is filled."

He raised his hand. In his palm sat a crystal vial, swirling with liquid midnight. The last drops of pure, unbound magic, stolen from the Academy's heart.

"You see," Malachar said, "I don't need the dragon's permission. I only needed him to confirm the ritual. And now that I know the price—becoming the Source—I am more than willing to pay it."

He drank.

The world screamed.

The obsidian walls cracked as power surged into Malachar. His robes whipped in a wind that came from nowhere and everywhere. His eyes turned solid black, then began to glow with a sickly, greedy light.

"No," Aurelion roared, rising to his full, terrible height. "Fool! You drink without binding! The magic will—"

Malachar gestured.

The dragon slammed against the wall, pinned by invisible force. Ancient scales cracked. Golden blood dripped onto the stone.

"Thank you for the lesson," Malachar said, his voice echoing with layers of thunder. "But I prefer my power uncontained."

He turned to Kael and Lyra, and smiled.

"Run along, children. Go tell the world its new god has arrived. Or stay, and watch me rewrite the laws of reality beginning with your—"

The pillar behind him shattered.

Not from Malachar's power, but from within.

Something emerged from the broken amber. Something that had been sleeping inside the dragon's seal, older than Aurelion, older than the Dying itself.

It had no shape Kael's mind could hold. It was absence given form, hunger given teeth, and it looked at Malachar with a curiosity that was infinitely worse than anger.

"Oh," Malachar said, his godlike confidence cracking. "That's what was keeping the magic here."

The thing lunged.

And Kael realized, with horrible clarity, that Aurelion hadn't been guarding the secret of how to save magic.

He'd been guarding the reason magic was dying in the first place.

And now it was free.
