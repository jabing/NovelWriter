The crystal pickaxe sparked violet against stone that shouldn't exist.

Kael stumbled backward, coughing through his dust mask as afterimages danced across his vision. The tunnel wall ahead wasn't granite anymore. It was something blacker than soot, carved with spirals that seemed to turn inward on themselves, hungry for light.

"Section twelve-b," he wheezed, pressing his palm against the throbbing surface. "Collapsed tunnel, my ass."

His fingers trembled. Archmage Vex had sent him here to vanish. That much was obvious. Three weeks mapping the Caverns of Echoing Stone—three weeks of cold rations and stale air—wasn't punishment for correcting the High Theorist's math in open council. It was execution by tedium. Or cave-in. Whichever came first.

But this...

Kael closed his eyes and reached for his reservoir. It was pitiful, really. A puddle where other mages held lakes. Archivist-class sensitivity, the evaluators had called it. Useless for combat. Barely sufficient for illumination.

Yet precise. Precise enough to feel the wrongness thrumming through the bedrock.

"There's a rhythm," he whispered.

Boom.

Not the drip of water. Not the shift of tectonic plates. This was organic. A pulse measured in centuries rather than seconds, vibrating through glyphs that hadn't seen torchlight in ten thousand years.

Kael's illumination spell flickered. The spirals weren't carved. They were grown—crystalline veins pulsing with amber light that matched the beat shaking his teeth.

He should climb back. Three miles of rope ladder to where Supervisor Merrick waited with his sneer and his ledger. Report the anomaly. Wait for proper clearance forms in triplicate.

Instead, Kael pressed his ear to the stone.

The pulse quickened.

Like a heart recognizing a heartbeat.

The wall dissolved.

Not crumbled. Not broke. It simply ceased to exist as a barrier, revealing a passage that breathed warm air smelling of ozone and old, old iron. The darkness beyond his light sphere was absolute, hungry. But the pulse pulled him forward, a fishhook in his sternum, tugging him deeper into the Hollow Mountain's forgotten artery.

His boots crunched on something that wasn't rock.

Kael looked down. Bones. Dragon bones, by the curvature—ribs like ship hulls, vertebrae the size of wine barrels. But old. Older than the Academy. Older than the First Council.

"Stars above," he breathed.

The pulse grew louder.

The chamber opened like a mouth.

Kael's light spell hit the ceiling and scattered, refracted through a million crystalline fractures until the entire cavern glittered with trapped starlight. He gasped, stumbling backward over more bones.

Not a cavern. A prison.

And inside it, breathing.

The dragon filled the space like a mountain range. Its scales, what remained of them, were the color of burnt gold and dying sunlight. But they hung loose on a frame that had once been magnificent, now reduced to ridges of bone and sagging flesh.

Emaciated didn't cover it. This was starvation written across a continent.

Thick cords of crystallized magic pierced the dragon's wings, its torso, its skull—translucent leech-lines that pulsed with the same amber glow as the tunnel glyphs. With each pulse, the dragon shuddered, and the crystals brightened, drinking deep.

"Ley-lines," Kael breathed.

He'd memorized their paths in Academy texts. The rivers of power flowing beneath the continent, feeding the Mage Council's towers, the floating cities, the weather-wards keeping famine at bay. The source of all civilization. The foundation of progress.

They weren't rivers.

They were straws.

The dragon's eye opened.

It was larger than Kael's entire body, a vertical slit of molten bronze that shouldn't have held consciousness. Not after this long. Not after this pain. But intelligence burned there. Ancient, weary, and suddenly—sharply—focused.

*You hear it,* a voice said inside Kael's skull.

Not words. Concepts with weight. The pressure of mountains against his frontal lobe.

Kael's knees hit the stone. His pathetic reservoir of magic evaporated, siphoned toward the creature by sheer gravitational pull.

"I—" His voice cracked. "I didn't mean to—"

*Mean to?* The dragon's thought carried bitter amusement, dry as desert wind. *You think this is accident, little mage? That your light spells burn my liver by happenstance?*
