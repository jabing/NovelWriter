The mana crystal shattered three inches from Master Elandra's palm.

Not exploded. Not cracked. Shattered like glass struck by an invisible hammer, its azure luminescence bleeding into gray smoke before the fragments hit the marble floor.

Five hundred students gasped as the Great Hall's temperature plummeted. Frost spiderwebbed across the windows, then evaporated into nothing.

"Stay calm," Elandra commanded, though her voice wavered. "The fading is... accelerating."

Kael Vane gripped the bench until his knuckles whitened. He wasn't watching the dissolving magic. He was watching the *pattern*.

The smoke didn't drift randomly. It spiraled upward, threading through the vaulted ceiling toward the Observatory Tower. Toward something hungry.

"Did you see that?" Lyra Ashford whispered beside him. Her notebook lay forgotten, equations she'd spent months calculating now useless as the fundamental laws unraveled.

"The direction," Kael murmured. "It's not fading. It's being—"

"Taken," Lyra finished. Her brown eyes widened behind her spectacles. "Kael, nothing should be able to consume raw mana. Not without a focusing crystal the size of a mountain."

Master Elandra raised her hands, attempting to conjure a stabilization ward. Her fingers sparked violet, then fizzled. For a heartbeat, the aging professor looked terrified.

"Class dismissed," she croaked. "Everyone to your dormitories. Now."

The hall erupted into panic. Students stampeded toward the exits, their own failing enchantments sputtering around them like dying fireflies. A girl's levitation charm gave out three feet from the ground. She landed hard, sobbing.

Kael didn't move.

"You're thinking something reckless," Lyra said. It wasn't a question.

"The spiral." Kael pointed at the ceiling. Faint traces of gray smoke still clung to the rafters, forming a vortex that pointed unerringly upward. "Something's pulling magic from the source. If we can get to the Observatory—"

"We'll die," Lyra said. "Or worse, we'll find whatever is killing magic and it'll notice us."

"Elandra said the fading is accelerating." Kael finally turned to her, seeing the fear she hid beneath her methodical exterior. "At this rate, there won't be magic left by dawn. No healing spells. No protective wards. The Academy sits on a leyline nexus, Lyra. When the failsafes break..."

He didn't finish. He didn't need to.

Lyra closed her notebook with a decisive snap. "Five minutes. If we don't find answers, we run."

They slipped through the chaos, dodging panicked third-years and faculty trying to maintain order. The stairwell to the Observatory was locked, but Kael had picked that particular lock a hundred times during midnight research sessions.

The mechanism clicked.

The door swung open onto madness.

The Observatory dome, usually transparent to reveal the stars, had turned opaque black. Not painted. Not covered. *Void*. The darkness moved like oil on water, pressing against the glass.

In the center of the room, surrounded by shattered astronomical equipment, lay Aurelion.

The great dragon filled the circular space, his silver scales dulled to the color of tarnished coin. His ribcage heaved with desperate, rattling breaths. Blood—thick, golden, and luminous—pooled beneath his claws.

"You're early," the dragon rumbled. His voice, usually like thunder over mountains, sounded like stones grinding in a dry riverbed.

Kael stepped forward, ignoring Lyra's restraining hand. "The magic isn't fading. It's being pulled here. To you."

"Clever boy." Aurelion's golden eye cracked open, the vertical pupil narrowing to a slit. "Not to me. Through me. I am the dam holding back the flood. And I am breaking."

Lyra's analytical mind seized the information. "The dragons are conduits. Leylines flow through you. If you're dying, the magic has nowhere to go—it should dissipate naturally. Unless..." She paled. "Something's siphoning it before it can recycle."

"Malachar," Kael spat.

The name hung in the air like poison.

Aurelion attempted to rise, but his legs buckled. More golden blood spilled. "The Headmaster has found the Root. The first magic. He thinks to consume it and become eternal."

"He's killing you to do it," Kael said.

"He's killing *everything*." Aurelion's tail thrashed weakly, toppling a telescope. "When the last dragon dies, the Root opens. Unlimited power for whoever holds the key. But the world will bleed magic until it becomes a husk. No healing. No growth. No life."

Kael approached the dying dragon. Up close, he could see the cracks in Aurelion's scales—cracks that pulsed with sickly green light, the color of Malachar's corrupted essence.

"How do we stop him?"

"You don't." Aurelion's great head lowered until his snout nearly touched Kael's chest. "I do. But I require a vessel. A bridge to channel the Root safely, to seal it rather than consume it."

Lyra grabbed Kael's arm. "No. Whatever you're implying—"

"I volunteer," Kael said.

"You don't understand the cost," Aurelion growled. "To become the bridge, you must accept the Root into your soul. You will live as long as magic exists. Centuries. Millennia. Watching everyone you love turn to dust while you remain, a fixture, a battery—"

"How long do we have if I don't?"

Aurelion's silence was answer enough.

Kael looked at Lyra. Really looked at her—the way her hair escaped its braid, the ink stain on her collar from too many sleepless study sessions, the determination in her eyes that matched his own.

"I can't let him win," Kael said softly.

Lyra's grip tightened. "Then we do it together. You said a bridge needs two anchors."

"The risk—"

"Is ours to share," she interrupted. Her voice shook, but her chin lifted. "Methodical, remember? You provide the courage, I provide the plan."

Aurelion's eyes gleamed with ancient sorrow. "Brave children. So terribly brave."

The Observatory door exploded inward.

Malachar stood in the wreckage, his academic robes replaced by armor forged from void-metal. His face, once distinguished and scholarly, had stretched into something inhuman—too many teeth, too many eyes reflecting the green corruption.

"How touching," the Headmaster said. His voice layered over itself, a choir of whispers. "The dragon and his pets, preparing to sacrifice themselves."

He raised his hand. The air crystallized into blades of frozen mana.

"I think not," Malachar smiled. "I've waited sixty years for this. I won't be denied by a boy with more heart than sense."

Kael pushed Lyra behind him. "Run."

"Not happening," she whispered back. She pulled a vial from her pocket—distilled starlight, volatile and rare. "Distract him."

Aurelion roared. Despite his wounds, the sound shook the tower, sending Malachar stumbling.

Kael charged.

He didn't have magic—his spells would fail like everyone else's—but he had momentum and rage. He tackled Malachar's midsection, driving the corrupted Headmaster into the broken telescope.

The void-metal armor absorbed the impact. Malachar backhanded Kael with casual cruelty, sending him sprawling across the floor. Stars burst in Kael's vision.

"You could have been my successor," Malachar said, walking forward. Green light gathered in his palm. "Instead, you'll be compost."

Lyra threw the vial.

It shattered against Malachar's chest, unleashing pure photonic force. The Headmaster screamed as his armor sizzled, smoke rising from the wound. It wouldn't hold him long—seconds, maybe.

"Now!" Lyra shouted.

Aurelion lunged. Not at Malachar, but at Kael. The dragon's forehead slammed against Kael's chest, and suddenly Kael was burning from the inside out.

The Root opened.

Kael saw everything—the birth of stars, the first spell ever cast, the web of leylines connecting every living thing. It was too much. It was glorious. It was agony.

He felt Lyra's hand find his. She was screaming, or maybe he was. Their souls touched the Root together.

Malachar lunged for them, hands outstretched to tear the power away.

But Aurelion was faster.

The ancient dragon interposed himself between the children and the monster, accepting Malachar's strike through his already-broken heart.

"Take them," Aurelion commanded the magic. "Make them eternal. Protect—"

Malachar's hand closed around the dragon's spine. He began to consume Aurelion's essence, green light battling silver.

Kael felt the Root's power surging through him, but it was incomplete. One anchor held—himself—but Lyra's grip was slipping. She wasn't strong enough. The power was burning her away.

He had to choose.

Save Lyra, and let Malachar consume Aurelion, becoming a god of void and ending the world.

Or complete the binding, accepting the full power of the Root, becoming the eternal vessel Aurelion warned him about—and watching Lyra die in his arms as the magic consumed her mortal soul.

Malachar laughed as Aurelion's scales began to turn gray.

"Choose, boy," the monster hissed. "Choose who you save. That's the only power any of us ever really have."

Kael looked at Lyra. Her eyes were closed, her breath shallow.

The magic demanded its price.

And Kael realized, with terrible clarity, that there was a third option—one that would damn them both, but might save everything else.

If he couldn't share the burden, and he couldn't bear it alone...

He could break it.

Kael reached not for Lyra's hand, but for the fractured, bleeding edge of Aurelion's dying soul. He grabbed the dragon's essence and the Root's power together, and instead of binding them to himself, he *shattered* the connection.

The Observatory exploded with light.

The last thing Kael heard before the void took him was Lyra screaming his name, and Malachar's howl of rage as unlimited magic became unlimited chaos, wild and unclaimed and free for anyone brave enough to seize it.
