The spell shattered at exactly midnight.

Kael Vane dove sideways as the containment field above the Restricted Archives fractured into jagged golden shards. Gravity inverted with a sickening lurch. Books tore themselves from iron shelves, pages fluttering like dying moths, suspended in mid-air by magic that was no longer obeying its own rules.

"Get down!" Lyra Ashford grabbed his collar.

She dragged him behind a stone lectern as a heavy tome on Advanced Chronomancy exploded. Not with fire—with absence. The pages didn't burn. They simply ceased to exist, leaving behind a hollow scream of displaced air.

Kael's skin prickled. This wasn't normal spell degradation. This was the Fade accelerating.

"Three containment circles just collapsed simultaneously," Lyra whispered. Her fingers traced rapid patterns in the air, trying to stabilize the ambient mana. Blue light flickered at her fingertips, then guttered out like a candle in a hurricane. "Something's pulling it. Actively drinking the magic dry."

Above them, the floating books began to fall. But slowly. Too slowly. They hung in the air like flies in amber, caught between the world's memory of magic and the terrifying reality of its absence.

Kael reached into his jacket. The Dragon's Mark on his palm pulsed hot against his ribs—a burning star-shaped scar that Aurelion had given him three weeks ago. It sensed the dying magic. It hungered for it, or perhaps mourned it. He still couldn't tell which.

"We need to reach the Central Anchor," Kael said. "If the Archives are failing, the Academy's main leyline is—"

A deafening crack split the air.

The floor beneath them buckled. Stone rippled like water as the structural reinforcement spells unraveled. Kael grabbed Lyra's hand. Her grip was steady, despite the terror in her gray eyes. They ran.

The grand chamber of the Restricted Archives had once glowed with soft, perpetual starlight. Now it was a graveyard of half-realized enchantments. A spectral librarian—just a residual memory of a summoning—reached for Kael as he passed. Its fingers dissolved into gray mist before it could touch him.

"Left!" Lyra shouted.

They turned down a corridor that shouldn't exist. The Academy's architecture was shifting as the spatial binding spells failed. Walls breathed. Stairs led into ceilings. Kael's Mark flared, burning a path through the confusion, pointing true north toward the Central Spire.

"How fast is it spreading?" Kael gasped as they sprinted through a doorway that opened into the wrong building entirely—they were suddenly in the West Dormitory kitchens.

"Too fast," Lyra said. She skidded to a halt beside a window. Her breath fogged the glass. "Kael. Look."

Outside, the sky was wrong.

The Aurora Arcana—the shimmering magical veil that had protected the Academy for three centuries—was bleeding. Ribbons of emerald and violet light drained upward into a point of absolute blackness hovering above the Headmaster's Tower. It looked like a tear in the fabric of reality.

Someone was weaponizing the Fade.

"Malachar," Kael breathed.

The door to the kitchen exploded inward.

Headmaster Vex Malachar stood in the smoking doorway, his academic robes transformed into something darker. Living shadow wove between the threads. In his right hand, he held a device that made Kael's soul recoil—a crystalline sphere filled with compressed, screaming light.

"Curious," Malachar said. His smile was gentle. His eyes were not. "The dragon's pet and his clever little assistant. I had hoped the Archives collapse would keep you occupied longer."

Lyra stepped in front of Kael. Her hands moved into a defensive casting stance, though Kael could see her fingers trembling. The ambient mana was too thin. Any spell she tried would fizzle, or worse, backlash.

"You've been harvesting the Fade," Kael said. He could feel it now—the sphere in Malachar's hand pulled at his Mark like a magnet pulls iron. "You're not trying to stop magic from dying. You're killing it faster to collect the residue."

"Refined, not collected," Malachar corrected. He stepped into the kitchen. The tile beneath his boots cracked and turned gray as he drained the residual enchantments from the floor. "Magic isn't fading naturally, boy. It's being displaced. Pushed out. And in the transition between existence and void, there is power. Raw, beautiful power."

Kael's hand went to his dagger. Useless against Malachar, but he needed to buy time. The Mark was screaming at him now, a silent roar only he could hear. *Run*, it said. *Run to the Spire. Run to Aurelion.*

"You're insane," Lyra said. "If you drain the leylines completely, the Academy will fall into the Void. Thousands will die."

"Progress requires sacrifice," Malachar said softly. He raised the sphere.

The light in the kitchen died. Not metaphorically—every candle, every magical luminescence, every spark of warmth vanished. Absolute darkness swallowed them. Kael heard Lyra gasp. Then he felt the cold. A cold that didn't lower temperature but removed the concept of heat entirely.

Kael's Mark erupted with silver flame.

The light didn't illuminate the room so much as define it, carving boundaries back into a reality that was trying to unmake itself. Malachar recoiled, his perfect composure cracking.

"Impossible," the Headmaster hissed. "The Dragon's Kiss shouldn't ignite without ambient mana to fuel it."

"Aurelion is older than your leylines," Kael said. He grabbed Lyra's hand. The silver fire enveloped her too, warm and alive against the Void-cold. "And he's not running out of magic. He's just been saving it."

They ran.

Behind them, Malachar screamed orders in a language that hurt to hear. Shadows detached from the corners—his Void-touched enforcers, students who had volunteered for power and received emptiness instead. They flowed after Kael and Lyra like spilled ink.

The Academy had become a labyrinth of shifting corridors and decaying wonders. Statues wept stone tears as their animation spells died. The Great Clock in the central hall tolled thirteen times, then shattered, gears flying upward to join the swirling vortex of stolen magic above the tower.

Kael and Lyra burst onto the Spire Bridge—a narrow span of crystal that connected the main campus to the Dragon's Peak. Below, the Endless Ocean roared against the cliffs, but the sound was muted, distant. The world was losing its vibrancy, becoming a sketch of itself.

"Almost there," Lyra panted. Her analytical mind was still working, even as she ran for her life. "Kael, if Malachar is harvesting the Fade, he needs a receptacle. That sphere. If we can break it—"

"It'll release all the stolen magic at once," Kael finished. "It might restore the leylines, or it might tear the Academy apart."

"Or," Lyra said, her eyes narrowing, "it might complete whatever ritual he's actually performing. I don't think he's just collecting power, Kael. I think he's opening a door."

The Dragon's Peak loomed ahead. The ancient stone spire where Aurelion dwelt had gone silent weeks ago, the mighty roars that once shook the mountains replaced by a worrying stillness. But now, as they approached, Kael heard something. A heartbeat. Slow. Irregular. Pained.

They reached the massive iron doors. Kael pressed his burning Mark against the seal. The metal groaned, recognizing the dragon's touch, and swung inward.

The interior of the spire was wrong.

Aurelion, the ancient gold wyrm who had taught at the Academy for eight centuries, lay coiled in the center of the vast chamber. But he was translucent. Fading. His scales, once bright as captured sunlight, were turning clear as glass. Through his fading form, Kael could see the bones of the mountain itself.

"Aurelion," Kael whispered.

The dragon's eye opened. It was still gold, still wise, but the pupil was expanding, bleeding black.

"You came," Aurelion rumbled. His voice echoed in Kael's mind, not his ears. The physical world could no longer carry the weight of dragon-speech. "Good. The Fade has reached the Source."

Lyra knelt beside the dragon's massive head, her healer's instincts overriding her fear. She placed a hand on his snout. "You're not fading. You're being drained. Like the leylines."

"Yes," Aurelion said. A shudder ran through his ethereal form. "Malachar has found the Crooked Path. He believes he can become a god by consuming the death of magic itself."

Kael knelt opposite Lyra. The silver fire of his Mark danced between them, casting long shadows. "How do we stop him? Tell me there's a way to reverse this."

"There is," Aurelion said. His gaze locked onto Kael's. "But you will not like it, young Vane. The Fade is not an ending. It is a transfer. Magic is not dying—it is being moved. Stolen. To stop the thief, one must become the new vessel."

"Become the vessel?" Lyra asked. "You mean... absorb the magic back?"

"Not the magic," Aurelion said. His form flickered, growing more transparent by the second. "The Source. The Heart of the World. It has been without a guardian for three centuries, hidden, waiting. Malachar seeks to claim it. But it can only be held by one with the Dragon's Mark."

Kael's blood ran cold. He looked at his palm, at the silver star burning there. "You mean me."

"You, or Malachar," Aurelion said. "If he claims the Source, he will unmake reality and rebuild it in his image. If you claim it..." The dragon paused. His form was nearly gone now, just a suggestion of golden light. "If you claim it, you will save the world. But you will become the world. Your humanity will burn away like morning mist. You will be eternal. Alone."

The doors to the spire exploded inward.

Malachar strode through the smoke, the crystalline sphere blazing with stolen starlight. Behind him, the Void-touched enforcers filled the doorway, blocking all escape. The Headmaster's face was twisted with triumph and something else—hunger.

"Thank you for confirming my theories, old worm," Malachar said. He raised the sphere. It pulsed in rhythm with Kael's Mark. "I suspected the boy was the key. Now, step aside, Kael. Let the dragon die, and give me what is mine."

Aurelion's voice whispered in Kael's mind, urgent and final: *Choose now, Vane. Take the Source, or let him have it. But choose quickly. Once I am gone, the doorway closes forever.*

Kael looked at Lyra. She was watching him, eyes wide with understanding. She knew what the choice meant. If he took the Source, he would save them all. And he would lose her. Lose everyone. Become something other, something eternal and distant, forever guarding the flow of magic but never touching it, never loving it, never being human again.

The sphere in Malachar's hand blazed brighter.

"Choose," Malachar commanded.

Kael reached out his hand, the Mark burning white-hot now, and made his choice.
