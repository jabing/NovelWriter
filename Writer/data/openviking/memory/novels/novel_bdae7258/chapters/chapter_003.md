Kael pressed his eye against the ventilation slit, watching twelve robed figures decide the end of the world.

Below, in the Crystal Amphitheater, the High Council of Archons stood in a circle of hovering light. Their faces were hidden behind masks of polished star-glass. At the center, hovering above the well that descended into the Deep Source, floated Malachar.

The Chief Archon wore robes of midnight blue that drank the light. He gestured, and a holographic projection bloomed above the assembly—a shimmering diagram of Aurelion, the Last Dragon, bound in seven chains of pure force.

“For three centuries, we have sipped from the cup of power,” Malachar said. His voice carried the weight of coercion magic, making the air shimmer. “We have treated the symptoms of the Fade with palliative care. Today, we cure the disease.”

Kael’s stomach twisted. Beside him in the narrow ventilation shaft, Lyra Ashford gripped his forearm, her nails digging in. Her breath came in short, terrified bursts.

“The Ascension Ceremony,” Malachar continued, spreading his hands. The diagram shifted, showing Aurelion’s essence being drawn upward like smoke into twelve waiting vessels. “We shall harvest the Deep Source completely. The dragon dies. We transcend. Magic becomes ours to command directly, permanently, without the weakening intermediary of the Wells.”

One of the elder Archons—Veyra, her mask cracked with age—stepped forward. “The creature’s death will release enough raw thaumaturgic energy to elevate us to godhood. But what of the Fade? The Well readings show—”

“The Wells lie,” Malachar snapped. The temperature in the chamber dropped ten degrees. “Aurelion has been manipulating the gauges, hoarding his strength. When we consume him, we consume true immortality. The Fade ends because magic will become us.”

Kael felt the Mark on his chest burn. It was a brand in the shape of a dragon’s eye, given to him three nights ago when Aurelion had first whispered through his dreams. Now it pulsed in agony, a warning.

“They’re going to kill him,” Lyra whispered. Her eyes were wide behind her spectacles. “Kael, if they drain the Deep Source, the ambient magic will collapse. My calculations—the equations don’t support transcendence. They support extinction.”

“Vote,” Malachar commanded.

Twelve crystal orbs floated from the Archons’ sleeves. One by one, they turned black.

Unanimous.

Kael’s vision swam. He saw his future—the comfortable life of a ranked Magister, the salary, the safety—evaporating like morning frost. He saw the world without magic. Dead. Cold. Silent.

“We have to go,” Kael breathed.

They crawled backward through the shafts, metal screeching against their shoulders. The sound was lost beneath the thunder of ceremonial drums below. They dropped into the maintenance tunnels, running, until the humid breath of the Deep Source filled their lungs.

The dragon waited.

Aurelion filled the cavern.

His scales were the color of dying embers, rust and gold and ancient, ancient grey. Seven massive chains, each link inscribed with a thousand binding runes, pierced his flesh and anchored him to the walls of the Heart-Chamber. His wings were pinned. His tail coiled around a crystalline pillar that pulsed with stolen light.

He was beautiful.

He was dying.

“You heard.” The dragon’s voice was not sound but resonance, vibrating in Kael’s teeth. Aurelion’s eye—vertical, golden, deeper than time—opened slowly. “The fools think to eat the heart and keep the body alive.”

Lyra stepped forward, her scholar’s instincts warring with her terror. “The conservation of thaumaturgic energy—if they drain you, the ambient field collapses. But Malachar claims the Fade is a deception.”

“The Fade is real.” Aurelion shifted. The chains groaned. Sparks of raw magic—blue-white and wild—flickered from his wounds. “I am not merely a battery to be drained, little scholar. I am the Heart. The first principle. When the world was young, magic did not exist until my kind sang it into being. We are the source code. Kill me, and you delete the program.”
