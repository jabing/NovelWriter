The containment field shattered at exactly midnight.

Kael Vane threw himself sideways as azure sparks rained down like burning hail. The Grand Conjuration Chamber's marble floor cracked beneath the impact, fissures spiderwebbing where pure magical force struck stone.

"Kael!" Lyra Ashford grabbed his collar, hauling him behind an overturned lectern. "The resonance is collapsing!"

He tasted copper and ozone. Above them, the experimental mana core they'd been studying spun wildly, shedding streamers of dying light. This wasn't supposed to happen. The core was stabilized, ancient, a relic from when magic still flowed thick as blood through the world.

Now it was screaming.

"It's accelerating," Lyra shouted over the windstorm of escaping essence. Her dark hair whipped across her face, her fingers already sketching containment sigils in the air. Golden light trailed from her fingertips, but the symbols frayed before fully forming. "The Fade—it's eating the binding spells!"

Kael drew his athame. The blade trembled in his grip, not from fear, but from the hollow emptiness spreading through the room. Magic was bleeding out, dissolving into nothing. He could feel it in his bones, that terrible silence where the world's song used to be.

"We need to ground it," he yelled.

"There's nothing left to ground it *to*!"

The core exploded.

Not with fire, but with absence. A silent shockwave of void rippled outward, and every magical light in the Academy extinguished simultaneously. Kael's protective charms turned to dust on his wrists. Lyra's defensive wards collapsed like wet paper.

In the sudden darkness, they heard footsteps.

Calm. Measured. Deliberate.

"How predictable," said Dean Malachar. His voice cut through the ringing silence like a scalpel. "The two brightest students, tampering with forces they barely comprehend."

Kael rose slowly, positioning himself between the dean and Lyra. In the moonlight streaming through high windows, Malachar's silver hair gleamed like a crown of blades. His academic robes hung perfectly straight, untouched by the chaos that had just devastated the chamber.

"This wasn't our doing," Kael said. His voice sounded rough, even to himself. "The Fade hit the core. It destabilized on its own."

"Did it?" Malachar stepped over the cracked floor, his boots clicking against stone. He held something in his hand—a siphon crystal, dark and hungry. "Or did someone *attract* the decay? Someone carrying a particular... resonance."

Lyra's breath hitched. "You knew this would happen. You planted the core here to test us."

"I planted it," Malachar said softly, "to confirm a theory. The magic isn't simply dying, children. It's being drawn. Like water draining through a hole in a barrel." He pointed the crystal at Kael. "And you, Mr. Vane, are the hole."

Kael felt the words like a physical blow. "That's impossible."

"Is it? Your father worked the Aetherium Mines. Your mother studied the Old Draconic texts. You carry bloodlines on both sides that predate the Fade." Malachar's smile was gentle and terrible. "You are a catalyst. A walking absence where magic goes to die."

The crystal flared. Kael felt something tug inside his chest, a sickening pull that made his vision swim. Around him, the last residual sparks of the exploded core twisted in the air, bending toward him like iron filings to a magnet, then vanishing into his shadow.

"Stop it!" Lyra thrust her hand forward. A lance of pure force—sharp, precise, desperate—shot toward Malachar.

The dean flicked his wrist. The spell dissolved three feet from his face, unraveling into mist.

"Your technique is flawless, Miss Ashford. But quantity has a quality all its own." Malachar raised his other hand, and the darkness in the room congealed. "When there is no magic left to command, what use is perfect form?"

Shadows lunged.

Kael moved without thinking. He tackled Lyra, rolling them both toward the chamber's eastern archway. Cold dark energy sliced through the space where they'd stood, severing a stone pillar clean through. The ceiling groaned.

"Run," Kael whispered, hauling Lyra to her feet. "The dragon's passage. Now."

They sprinted into the corridor. Behind them, Malachar's laughter echoed, too knowing, too pleased.

The secret way to Aurelion's sanctum lay beneath the Academy's lowest cellars, behind a wall that wasn't there unless you knew how to ask.

Kael pressed his palm against damp stone, blood still roaring in his ears. The magic-word was simple—*Aevum*—but speaking it felt like swallowing glass. The Fade pressed against his mind, a pressure that wanted to crush the mystic syllables into silence.

The wall groaned and dissolved, revealing stairs spiraling down into depths the Academy's maps didn't acknowledge.

"He's right behind us," Lyra gasped. She clutched her ribs where Malachar's shadow had grazed her. Frost sparkled on her torn sleeve. "Kael, what he said... about you being a catalyst..."

"Later." He grabbed her hand, pulling her into the passage. The wall reformed behind them, but he knew it wouldn't stop Malachar for long. "We need answers. Aurelion has them."

They descended into earth that smelled of cinnamon and old lightning. The stairs ended in a cavern vast enough to swallow the Academy's great hall twice over. Stalactites of crystallized mana hung from the ceiling, but they were dark now. Dead.

In the center of the cavern, coiled around a pedestal of star-iron, waited Aurelion.

The dragon was smaller than the stories suggested, no bigger than a draft horse, but reality seemed to bend around his presence. His scales shifted between midnight blue and the color of forgotten memories. When he opened one amber eye, the weight of centuries pressed down upon them.

"You brought the storm, little sparks," Aurelion rumbled. His voice wasn't loud, but it filled the cavern like water filling a cup. "I smell the hollow-man on you. Malachar walks boldly now."

"He knows something about me," Kael said, stepping forward. The dragon's gaze pinned him like a butterfly to a board. "He says I'm causing the Fade. That I'm absorbing magic."

Aurelion uncoiled sinuously, moving with liquid grace. He circled Kael, nostrils flaring. Warm wind, smelling of desert storms and library dust, washed over them.

"Not absorbing," the dragon corrected. "Conducting. There is a difference, though the result appears the same."

Lyra stepped closer to Kael, her shoulder brushing his. "Explain. Please."

"The magic isn't dying," Aurelion said. He lifted a claw, and for a moment, a perfect sphere of golden light appeared above his palm. It pulsed, beautiful and alive. Then, slowly, it began to spiral downward, drawn toward Kael like water down a drain. "It is migrating. Fleeing."

"From what?" Kael demanded.

"From the world that broke its promise." Aurelion's eyes narrowed to slits. "Long ago, your kind and mine made a covenant. Magic was the bridge between thought and reality, sustained by belief and blood. But your ancestors grew greedy. They tried to bind the source itself, to own it."

The dragon gestured to the dark crystals above. "They failed. The source is wounded. It bleeds out through the thinnest places in reality. Through conduits. Through... openings."

"Through me," Kael whispered.

"Through you," Aurelion agreed. "You are the latest in a line of unintended doorways. Magic passes through you to somewhere else. Somewhere beyond."

Lyra gripped Kael's arm. "Then we close the door. There must be a way to seal him, to stop the migration—"

"There is," Aurelion interrupted. "But you will not like it."

Above them, the cavern ceiling cracked. Stone dust rained down. A voice—chillingly calm—filtered through the rock.

"Come now, children," Malachar called. His tone was that of a disappointed tutor. "Hiding with the beast won't save you. It only delays the inevitable."

Aurelion's wings spread, casting shadows that seemed to have weight. "The siphon-crystal he carries. It is a key. A terrible one. He intends not to stop the Fade, but to reverse it through forced extraction."

"Extraction from what?" Kael asked, though dread already pooled in his stomach.

"From me," Aurelion said softly. "Dragons are the oldest reservoirs of raw magic. If he drains me, he will have enough power to force the migration backward. To pull all escaping magic into himself. He would become a god of sorts. For a time."

"And then?" Lyra asked.

"And then the wound in the world tears wide open. The last of the magic hemorrhages out. Reality follows." Aurelion fixed Kael with a piercing stare. "Unless the doorway closes first."

Kael understood. The weight of it settled on his shoulders like a mountain. "I have to die."

"Kael, no—" Lyra turned to him, her eyes wide and bright with panic.

"There is another way," Aurelion said quickly. "But it requires sacrifice of a different kind. You must become the lock instead of the door. Bind the migration to your own life-force. Anchor the magic to your heartbeat."

"That would save the world," Kael said slowly. "But?"

"But you would become static," Aurelion whispered. "You would live, but magic would flow through you constantly, freezing you in time. Unaging. Unchanging. Watching everyone you love grow old and die while you remain... preserved. A living dam against the void."

The ceiling exploded inward.

Malachar descended on wings of shadow, the siphon crystal blazing like a black sun in his fist. Behind him, the air tore open, revealing a swirling vortex of hungry absence.

"How touching," the Dean said, landing lightly amidst the rubble. "The dragon offers you oblivion in slow motion. But I offer you power, Kael. Join me. Let us harvest the wyrm together and rule what remains of this dying world."

He extended his hand. In it lay a second crystal, clear and waiting.

"Choose," Malachar commanded. "The dragon's prison... or mine."
