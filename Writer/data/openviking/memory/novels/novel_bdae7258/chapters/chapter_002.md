The tunnel breathed.

Not wind—something older. Kael pressed his palm against the obsidian wall, feeling the pulse that had kept him awake for three nights straight. It matched his heartbeat.

Or rather, his heartbeat had learned to match its own.

"You return." The voice didn't come from the darkness ahead. It came from inside his skull, rich and resonant as struck bronze. "Good. The lies have been gnawing at you, haven't they, little mouse?"

Kael's light-spell flickered. The glyph he'd carved into his forearm—basic illumination, first-year stuff—sputtered as his mana responded to the dragon's proximity. His skin ithed. His teeth ached.

Five days ago, this sensation had terrified him.

Now it felt like coming home.

"Tell me," Kael whispered. His voice cracked. "Tell me what they did to you."

The darkness moved. Not sudden—unfurled. Aurelion's massive eye opened thirty feet ahead, vertical pupil dilating to drink in the pale blue glow of Kael's magic. The iris was molten gold, shot through with cracks of crimson where the sealing chains had bitten deep into the dragon's essence.

"Five centuries ago," Aurelion rumbled, and the stone itself seemed to remember, vibrating with resonance, "your kind did not steal magic. They shared it."

The air thickened. Kael gasped as his light-spell flared violently, burning from blue to blinding white. The cave walls dissolved.

He stood elsewhere.

**Sunlight.** Impossible, beautiful sunlight streaming through crystal spires. Kael felt grass beneath his boots—real grass, not the ash-gray moss of the Academy grounds. Power flowed through the air like wine, intoxicating, endless.

He saw them. Mages in silver robes, not the drab gray of modern archivists. They stood beside dragons not as masters before beasts, but as partners. Twin heartbeats synchronized. When a mage gestured, flame didn't just spark—it sang. When a dragon winged across the sky, the mage below breathed easier, sharing the burden of flight through their bond.

"This was the Covenant," Aurelion's voice whispered through the vision. "Symbiosis. We gave depth to their spells; they gave focus to our wildness. Magic was not fading then, little mouse. It was a river, and we were its banks."

The vision shattered.

Kael stumbled, retching, as the cave snapped back into focus. But now he saw the chains. Really saw them. Not iron—something worse. They were congealed shadow, solidified betrayal, boring into Aurelion's wrists and wings. Where they touched, the dragon's scales had gone gray, dead as old coal.

"The First Ten," Aurelion growled. Smoke boiled from his nostrils, carrying the scent of burning time. "They wanted immortality. Not the shared long-life of the Covenant, but eternal dominion. So they forged the Chains of Dominion from the broken bond itself."

Kael reached out before he could stop himself. His fingers brushed the nearest chain.

Pain exploded behind his eyes.

He saw the betrayal happen—the Ten standing in a circle where this very cave had been open sky, chanting words that twisted the air like poisoned roots. Aurelion roared as the first chain punched through his wing membrane. Not a physical blow. A spiritual severing.

They weren't just capturing him.

They were turning him into a well.

"They drink from me," Aurelion said, and now his voice was exhaustion itself. "Every spell cast in this Academy, every healing, every fire—it's my life they burn. Five centuries of slow consumption. The magic fades because I fade."

Kael yanked his hand back. His fingertips were blistered, but the pain felt distant. "The fading magic... it's because you're dying."

"Dying?" Aurelion laughed, and stalactites cracked overhead. "I am being *devoured*. And when I am empty, little mouse, the world will go dark."

Kael backed away, chest heaving. His light-spell died completely, but he didn't need it anymore. He could see Aurelion in the dark now. Really see him—not the monster from the textbooks, but the hollowed-out heart of the world.

"I have to tell—"

"Tell?" The dragon's eye narrowed. "Tell Archivist Sylas? Tell the Head Archimandrite? They know, Kael. They have always known. Why do you think the penalty for 'dragon-madness' is death?"

Kael froze.

Footsteps echoed from the tunnel entrance.

Not the slow patrol of guards. Quick, certain steps. Someone who knew the caves.

"Get out," Aurelion hissed, and the chains rattled with sudden violence. "Now. He cannot find you here."

Kael ran.

---

He burst from the hidden passage behind the eastern archive stacks, gasping, his gray apprentice robes torn at the shoulder. The familiar smell of dust and preservation spells hit him like a wall.

Too normal. Too safe.

The footsteps followed.

"Kael."

He spun, back pressed against the marble shelves.

Archivist Sylas stood three rows down, holding a lantern that burned with steady, artificial flame. The old man's face was usually a map of patience—kind eyes, ink-stained fingers, the perpetual smell of rosemary tea. Now those eyes were hard as agate.

"You've been below," Sylas said. Not a question.

"I was just—research," Kael stammered. His blistered hand hid behind his back. "The crystallography vaults, I thought I heard—"

"Don't." Sylas set the lantern down with a click that echoed like a hammer on stone. He stepped closer, and Kael saw the tremor in his hands. Not anger. Fear. "I smell sulfur on your breath, boy. I see the dilation in your eyes. You've been listening."

Kael's mouth went dry. "To what?"

"You know."

They stared at each other. The silence of the library pressed down, heavy with secrets.

"It's real," Kael whispered. "All of it. The Covenant, the betrayal, the chains—Sylas, they're killing him. The magic is fading because they're *eating* him alive."

Sylas moved faster than a man his age should.

His hand clamped over Kael's mouth, pressing him back into the bookshelf. Tomes tumbled—*History of the First Age, The Dominion Charter, Standard Thaumaturgical Theory*—crashing around them like dead birds.

"Never speak those words," Sylas hissed. His breath was hot against Kael's ear. "Not here. Not where the walls have ears enchanted into the stone."

Kael struggled, but the old man's grip was iron.

"Dragon-madness," Sylas said, and the term sounded like a curse. "It's real, Kael. Not as they teach it—the madness isn't loving dragons. It's *knowing* them. Letting them inside your head. The Archmages burned the last student who spoke of 'partnerships' three decades ago. Burned him alive in the central plaza while the Ten drank wine and watched."

Kael stopped struggling.

Sylas released him, stepping back. His shoulders slumped. "I thought you were different. Smarter. You have potential—real potential, not the parlor trick nonsense they teach now. Don't throw it away for a monster's sweet lies."

"He's not—"

"Aurelion is a *well*," Sylas snapped. "A battery. That's all that's left. Whatever he was, the Covenant is dead. Has been dead for five hundred years. The only choice now is whether you join him in obscurity, or survive."

Kael looked at his mentor—the man who had taught him to read the old scripts, who had smuggled him extra bread during study sessions. The man who had never once questioned why the advanced texts were all locked away.

"You knew," Kael realized. "All this time. You knew the history was wrong."

Sylas turned away. "Go to bed, Kael. Scrub the sulfur from your skin. Forget what you heard in the dark."

"I can't."

"Then you're already dead." Sylas picked up his lantern, and the light carved deep shadows into his face. "Just like the Thirteen."

Kael blinked. "Who?"

But Sylas was already walking away, his boots whispering against the stone. "Lock the east stacks behind you. And Kael? If I find you've gone below again, I'll report you myself. Better a quick death by fire than what the Ten do to those who wake the blood."

He vanished around the corner, leaving Kael alone with the humming silence of ten thousand lies.

---

Kael didn't sleep.

He sat in his narrow dormitory cell until the moon reached its apex, turning the stone floor silver. Then he moved.

The Restricted Archive occupied the lowest level of the Academy, behind doors that required both key and blood-oath. But Kael had spent three years as Sylas's assistant. He knew the servant's passages. He knew which stones were loose in the wall.

He slipped through the gap like smoke.

The Restricted section breathed differently than the upper library. The air tasted of ozone and old violence. Here, the books weren't organized by subject. They were organized by danger.

Kael moved by the light of his palm—rekindled with a whisper, though it guttered weakly. The fading magic. He could feel it now, the way a sailor feels a storm in his joints. The well was running dry.
