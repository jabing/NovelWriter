The chains were eating the dragon alive.

Kael pressed his palm against the damp stone wall of the Academy's forbidden depths, watching violet light bleed from the iron links wrapped around Aurelion's throat. Each breath the great dragon took sent sparks of dying magic into the dark.

"You shouldn't be here, boy." Aurelion's voice rumbled like distant thunder, weak and cracking. Golden eyes, dim as guttering candles, fixed on Kael. "The Warden knows. He always knows."

"I'm not leaving." Kael's fingers trembled against his spell-casting focus—a cracked crystal that had once glowed sapphire bright. Now it flickered like a star about to wink out. "The Fade is accelerating. The eastern provinces have gone dark. Whatever Vane is doing to you—"

"He's harvesting me." Aurelion's wings twitched against the cavern floor, sending dust spiraling upward. "Siphoning draconic essence to postpone his own power's decay. Short-term gain for one. Catastrophic loss for all."

The air tasted of copper and ozone. Kael felt the familiar itch in his palms, the place where magic usually pooled warm and waiting. Now it felt like grasping smoke. The Fade had claimed his third-year illusions last week, turning a simple light cantrip into a wheezing ember.

Footsteps echoed from the stairwell.

Kael spun, crystal raised. The darkness coalesced into Professor Vane's silhouette, academic robes billowing with unnatural stillness. The professor's hands crackled with stolen power—purple-black lightning that shouldn't exist in a human mage's grasp.

"Kael Thornwick." Vane's smile was surgical. "Breaking seventeen Academy statutes. Trespassing in the Restricted Deep. Consorting with a Class-A Anomalous Entity." He clicked his tongue. "Expulsion seems insufficient."

"Vane." Kael stepped between the professor and the dragon. "You're killing him. And when he dies, the last anchor for the world's leylines dies with him."

"Precisely the point." Vane raised his hand. The stolen dragon-magic coalesced into a spear of shadow. "Why stabilize a failing system when one can simply... extract its remaining value?"

The spear launched.

Kael threw himself sideways. The projectile struck stone where he'd stood, dissolving bedrock into dust. He rolled to his knees, already weaving a defensive matrix. His fingers sketched the sigil for 'shield' in the air, but the magic caught halfway—stuttering, gasping, incomplete.

The barrier manifested as cobweb-thin glass.

Vane laughed. "You feel it, don't you? The emptiness? Magic is leaving us, boy. Has been for three centuries. I simply accept what sentimental fools deny."

"Then why steal his power if magic is doomed?" Kael scrambled behind a fallen column, heart hammering against his ribs.

"Because dragons remember." Vane's shadow stretched across the cavern, impossibly long. "They remember the First Word. The true name of creation. Aurelion knows how to weave new leylines, how to restore what was lost. But he won't share."

"And torture makes him more cooperative?" Kael peeked around the column. Aurelion had gone very still, watching with those ancient eyes.

"Dragons are stubborn." Vane summoned another spear. This one writhed with parasitic energy, tendrils drinking the ambient light from the chamber. "But everything breaks eventually. Even immortals."

Kael moved.

He didn't think. Thinking meant hesitation, and hesitation meant death. He poured every remaining drop of power into his legs—an enhancement charm so basic even the Fade couldn't corrupt it entirely—and launched himself across the cavern.

His shoulder caught Vane in the sternum.

They crashed against the wall. Vane's spell dissipated in a shriek of wasted energy. Kael's fist connected with the professor's jaw once, twice, before a blast of force sent him flying backward.

He hit the ground hard. Ribs screamed. Blood filled his mouth.

"Physical violence." Vane straightened, touching his split lip with genuine surprise. "How delightfully provincial. But you've exhausted your reserves, haven't you? Feel that hollowness in your chest?"

Kael tried to summon fire—a spark, anything—and found only cold ash where his power had lived.

Vane raised his hand for the killing strike.

"Enough."

The word wasn't loud, but it bent the air. Aurelion stirred, lifting his massive head despite the chains cutting into scaled flesh. The dragon's eyes blazed with sudden, terrible light.

"You've taken my blood, Warden. My pain. My dreams." Aurelion's voice deepened, resonating in Kael's bones. "But you cannot take my choice."

The chains shattered.

Not broke—unmade. The enchanted iron dissolved into motes of silver light, drifting upward like reverse rain. Aurelion rose to his full height, wings unfurling to scrape the cavern ceiling thirty feet above. The dragon was starlight given form, ancient and awful and beautiful.

Vane stumbled backward, face draining of color. "Impossible. The binding runes—"

"Were maintained by my consent." Aurelion stepped forward, and the stone beneath his claws bloomed with verdant moss—life returning to dead rock. "I allowed your theft, hoping humanity might use my essence wisely. You proved unworthy."

The dragon exhaled.

It wasn't fire. It was memory—the scent of primordial forests, the sound of oceans before time, the weight of mountains speaking their first names. Vane screamed as his stolen power fled his body, rushing back to its true source in streams of violet vapor.

The professor collapsed, unconscious before he hit the floor.

Silence returned to the deep places.

Kael sat up slowly, cradling his bruised ribs. "You could have done that... anytime?"

"Yes." Aurelion lowered his head until they were eye level. The dragon's breath smelled of cinnamon and thunderstorms. "And no. Freedom requires a witness, Kael Thornwick. Magic requires belief. You came when others fled. You stood when standing was impossible."

"The Fade," Kael said. "Can you stop it? Restore the leylines?"

Aurelion's eyes dimmed, sorrow replacing triumph. "I can restart the flow. But the engine requires fuel. A spark to ignite the dormant channels."

"What kind of spark?"

"Life," Aurelion whispered. "Specifically, a life bound to magic. A mage must surrender their arcane core—their very capacity for wonder—and feed it into the weave. One soul's extinction to rekindle the world's flame."

Kael felt the words settle in his stomach like stones.

"You're asking me to die?"

"No." Aurelion's wing curled protectively around Kael's shoulders, warm as a summer wind. "I'm asking you to choose. The ritual will take your magic. Every spell you've learned, every incantation, every possibility of power. You will become Null, forever cut off from the weave. But you will live."

"And if I refuse?"

"Then the Fade continues. Five years, perhaps ten, until the last ember dies. Civilizations built on sorcery will collapse. Wonder will become myth." Aurelion paused. "I cannot force you. I will not judge you."

Kael looked at his hands. They were still shaking from the fight, from the terror of facing Vane with empty palms. He remembered his first spell—calling light to a dark room at age seven. The joy of it. The rightness.

He thought of his sister in the eastern provinces, where the crops failed because weather-witches had gone silent. He thought of children who would never feel magic's warmth against their skin.

"I need time," Kael said.

"You have until dawn." Aurelion gestured toward a narrow passage leading upward. "The ritual chamber waits above. But Kael—"

The dragon's talon gently touched Kael's chest, directly over his heart. Magic flared, bright and sudden, showing Kael his own arcane core—a swirling galaxy of blue fire nestled in his soul.

"—know that if you do this, you will be the last sacrifice. The final price. Once the leylines reignite, the weave becomes self-sustaining. No one else need ever make this choice again."

Kael walked the passage in a daze.

He emerged onto the Academy's highest tower, where the wind whipped his torn uniform and the stars wheeled overhead. The ritual circle was already active, ancient stones humming with anticipation. The world's magic wasn't just dying; it was waiting.

Waiting for him to decide.

He raised his crystal focus one final time. It was dark now, truly dark. A dead thing in a dying world.

"Kael!"

He turned. Lyra, his fellow student, burst onto the tower, her face streaked with tears. She clutched a letter—the eastern seal visible even in starlight.

"It's your sister," she gasped. "The provinces... they've gone completely dark. The silence has taken them. They're not breathing, Kael. They're not breathing, and we can't wake them."

Kael looked from the letter to the ritual circle. The stones pulsed in rhythm with his heartbeat.

The choice was no longer abstract.

He stepped toward the circle, and the magic began to rise.
