The spell didn't just fail—it fed.

Kael felt the enchantment turn inside his chest, a hook of ice yanking backward through his ribs. The levitation sigil he'd traced in the air ignited not with the soft blue of accepted working, but with a hungry, sucking black.

"No," he gasped. "Break—"

The stone he'd been lifting didn't rise. Instead, Kael slammed to his knees.

Power drained out of him in a torrent. Not magical reserves—those had guttered days ago—but something deeper. His twentieth birthday had been last week. Now he felt nineteen. Eighteen. The spell was drinking his years.

"Release the working!" Lyra's voice cut through the roaring in his ears.

She vaulted the railing from the observation tier, her dark braid whipping behind her. Her hands moved in precise, methodical arcs—dispersal gestures, theoretical, untested. Because theory was all they had left. The old certainties were ash.

Kael tried to sever the connection. His fingers wouldn't obey. The black sigil pulsed, swelling like a leech gorging on blood.

Then Lyra's palm struck his shoulder.

Her touch burned with silver-white heat. The hunger spell shrieked—a sound like glass breaking underwater—and released him. Kael collapsed, gasping. The stone clattered harmlessly to the marble floor.

Silence filled the Arcanium.

Thirty students stared. Instructor Hale's chalk snapped in his grip. The old man's face had gone the color of old parchment.

"That working was Basic Levitation," Hale whispered. "First-year theory. It shouldn't be able to... it doesn't have the complexity to..." He trailed off, looking not at Kael, but at the air where the sigil had hung. "The ley lines aren't just thin anymore. They're predatory."

Kael spat copper. His hands looked the same—scarred, ink-stained—but they felt lighter. Wrong. As if someone had scooped out a handful of his future.

"Three months," Lyra said quietly. She was checking his eyes, her fingers cool against his jaw. Her grey eyes scanned methodically, clinically, but her breath came fast. "You lost three months. I watched your aura contract."

"Better than three years," Kael managed. His voice sounded hollow. "Thanks."

"Don't thank me." Her gaze flicked to the eastern archway. "Thank him for the demonstration."

Headmaster Malachar stood in the shadows beneath the arch. He hadn't been there when the working began. Now he leaned against the stone as if he'd watched the entire catastrophe unfold over tea.

His silver beard caught the failing light—real sunlight, weak and watery, nothing like the golden radiance that had bathed the Academy even five years ago. Magic was fading. Everyone knew it. But Malachar smiled like a man watching seeds he'd planted finally sprout.

"Intriguing," Malachar said. His voice carried the weight of old, heavy magic—compulsion weaves that still functioned while simpler spells failed. "The resistance threshold varies by bloodline. Vane here lasted twelve seconds. A commoner student would have been a husk."

"Headmaster," Hale stammered. "We must evacuate the eastern wing. If Basic workings have become parasitic—"

"We adapt," Malachar interrupted. He stepped into the light, and Kael saw that he carried something new at his hip. A sphere of absolute blackness, fist-sized, wrapped in chains of void-touched silver. The Siphon. It drank the torchlight. It made the air taste of graves. "The curriculum changes tomorrow. We teach survival, not sorcery."

Kael struggled to his feet, Lyra's grip steadying his elbow. "Survival? You watched me nearly die. You calculated it."

Malachar's smile didn't waver. "I observe. That is the purpose of a Headmaster." He turned, his robes swallowing the dim illumination. "Tomorrow, Vane. Come to my office. We must discuss your... unique resilience."

He left. The Siphon pulsed once, a heartbeat of darkness, and the temperature in the Arcanium dropped ten degrees.

"He's accelerating it," Lyra said when the footsteps faded. She pulled a notebook from her satchel—physical paper, ink, methods that didn't require the weave. Her pen scratched furiously. "The parasitic conversion. He's not just watching the magic die. He's feeding it."

Kael touched his chest. His heart hammered against ribs that felt too fragile, too new. "We need answers. Real ones. Not his lies."

"The dragon," Lyra said. She didn't look up from her notes. "Aurelion. The sealed archives mention a guardian beneath the keep. If anyone knows how magic truly works—how to stop this hunger—it's him."

"And if Malachar catches us?"

Lyra finally met his eyes. Hers were hard with determination, but underneath lay fear—not for herself, but for the calculation she'd just made. "Then we find out if the Siphon can eat dragonfire. Tonight, Kael. Before whatever he has planned for you tomorrow."
