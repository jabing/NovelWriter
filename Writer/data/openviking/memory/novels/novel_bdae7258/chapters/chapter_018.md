**Chapter 18: The Weight of Wings**

The spell shattered at 3:47 in the morning.

Kael threw himself backward as azure flames erupted from the cracked binding circle. Ancient manuscripts scattered like frightened birds. The smell of ozone and burning leather filled the Restricted Archives.

He landed hard on his elbows. Pain shot up his arms.

The flames didn't spread. Instead, they hovered, twisting into impossible geometries that hurt to observe directly. This wasn't supposed to happen. The ritual was meant to be subtle. A whisper. Not a scream.

"You've torn the fabric," said a voice like grinding millstones.

Kael scrambled backward until his shoulder blades hit the stone wall. "Aurelion?"

The flames coalesced into a shape—serpentine, translucent, wrong. The dragon's projection flickered as if viewed through water. His scales, usually radiant gold, appeared dull bronze. Fading.

"This is worse than I feared," Aurelion rumbled. The spectral head turned, regarding the Archives with eyes like dying stars. "Look beneath the surface, Kael. Stop staring at the fire. Look at what feeds it."

Kael forced his gaze down, through the floor stones.

His breath stopped.

Beneath the Academy's foundation lay a cavity larger than the entire campus. In its center, suspended by chains of pure light, pulsed a heart the size of a carriage house. Aurelion's true body—massive, wounded, pierced by a thousand silver threads that drained into the bedrock.

"They built their school around my cage," Aurelion whispered. The projection shuddered. "They didn't teach magic to preserve it. They taught it to farm it."

The vision snapped. Kael was staring at ordinary stone again, gasping.

The flames died, leaving scorch marks in the shape of wings.

* * *

Kael ran.

His boots hammered against spiral staircases that corkscrewed down into depths not shown on any map. The air grew hot. Thick. He passed doorways marked with warning seals—*Do Not Enter*, *Containment*, *Source*.

He didn't stop.

At the sub-basement level, the iron door should have been locked. It wasn't. It hung ajar, breathing warmth into the corridor.

"Aurelion?" Kael's voice cracked.

"Enter, child." The response was weak. Wet. "Before they realize you've seen."

Kael pushed through.

The chamber defied physics. It stretched upward and outward simultaneously, a cathedral of stone and root. And there, occupying the impossible space, lay Aurelion.

Not the proud beast Kael had met in dreams and whispered legends.

This dragon was skeletal. His golden scales had fallen away in patches, revealing musculature that flickered with dying embers. The silver threads—thousands of them—pierced his hide, disappearing into the stone. They pulsed rhythmically, sucking light from his veins.

Each pulse illuminated the Academy above.

"You look surprised," Aurelion said. One eye opened, slit-pupiled and clouded. "Did you think your professors conjured miracles from lectures? No. They siphon my marrow to light their lamps. They distill my blood to fuel their enchantments."

Kael stepped closer, horrified. "How long?"

"Three centuries." Aurelion's tail twitched, rattling chains that had no physical presence. "When magic first began fading, your ancestors didn't seek to heal the world. They sought to hoard what remained. They found my kind. The last dragons. One by one, we were bound. Harvested."

"You're the last," Kael realized.

"The final reservoir." Aurelion coughed. Sparks, not blood. "When I die, the lights go out. Forever."

Kael reached out, then hesitated. "I can free you. The threads—if I cut them—"

"You'll kill everyone you love." Aurelion's head lowered until his snout nearly touched Kael's chest. "The Academy floats because I dream of flying. The healers' wards work because I refuse to let pain exist uncontested. Cut the threads, and gravity remembers its weight. Sickness remembers its strength."

"But you're dying anyway!" Kael's voice broke. "The fading—it's accelerating. I can feel it in my spells. If you're going to die regardless—"

"Then I choose the manner of my death." Aurelion's eye gleamed. "The Dragon's Secret isn't a spell, Kael. It's a transfer. A lineage. My kind can pass our essence to another, but only one who carries the old blood. Only one who is willing to become the cage."

Kael went cold. "What are you saying?"

"I'm saying you have a choice, student of the Arcane." A new voice echoed from the doorway.

Kael spun.

Headmaster Vane stood silhouetted against the corridor's torchlight. His robes bore the silver-threaded embroidery of the High Council. In his hand, he held a crystal scalpel that hummed with extracted power.

"Choice one," Vane said, stepping into the chamber. "Aurelion transfers his remaining centuries into you. You become the Source. Immortal, eternal, suffering. The Academy continues. Magic survives. You replace him."

Kael's hands trembled. "And the second choice?"

"We complete the extraction tonight." Vane gestured. The silver threads brightened, pulsing faster. Aurelion groaned, a sound like mountains collapsing. "We take everything. Aurelion dies within the hour. The reservoir fills to maximum capacity. Magic returns to full strength for perhaps fifty years. Long enough for us to find another solution. Or another dragon."

"You don't know where to find another," Kael said.

"No." Vane smiled, thin and sharp. "Which is why Option One is so very practical."

Aurelion's voice was barely audible. "Don't listen, Kael. Break the threads. Let it end. Let me rest."

"But the world—" Kael started.

"The world adapted before magic," Aurelion hissed. "It will adapt again. It does not deserve your sacrifice. It does not deserve mine."

Vane raised the scalpel. The crystal blazed with stolen light. "He's lying, of course. Without magic, the floating kingdoms fall. The weather wards collapse. Billions die in the dark, Kael. Is one dragon's comfort worth genocide? Is your humanity worth their lives?"

The threads pulsed again. Aurelion screamed.

Kael looked at his hands. He looked at the threads. He looked at the dragon's eyes—ancient, kind, exhausted.

"I need a blade," Kael said quietly.

Vane smiled. "You choose wisely."

"No." Kael reached into his pocket and withdrew the silver letter opener he'd taken from the Archives.
