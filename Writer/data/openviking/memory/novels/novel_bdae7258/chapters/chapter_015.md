Chapter 15: The Fading Flame

The summoning circle shattered at exactly the wrong moment.

Silver light flickered across the examination chamber. Kael’s hands trembled as the containment runes faded from brilliant azure to sickly gray. The phoenix he’d conjured—supposed to be a harmless flame-sparrow—screamed as its wings dissolved into smoke.

“Hold the line!” Professor Vane shouted.

But Kael couldn’t hold what no longer existed.

The magic was *fading*.

Not failing. Not breaking. Simply ceasing to be, like breath on winter air. The phoenix’s form destabilized, its crystalline talons elongating into jagged void-tendrils. It lunged.

Kael threw himself sideways. The examination table exploded into splinters where he’d stood.

“Containment breach!” someone screamed.

Students stampeded for the exits. Through the chaos, Kael saw the creature’s eyes—pits of absolute black where fire should have danced. The Fade had taken something beautiful and left only hunger.

He reached for his reserves. The familiar warmth of his mana answered… then guttered out.

Empty.

Three years of study. Countless hours refining his channels. Gone in an instant, leaving him as mundane as a bricklayer.

The void-phoenix shrieked and turned toward the frozen first-years huddled by the door.

“No,” Kael whispered.

He grabbed the only weapon available—a broken casting rod—and charged.

The creature lunged. Kael swung blindly, expecting death.

Golden light erupted between them.

The impact threw Kael across the room. He crashed into a bookshelf, tomes cascading over his head. Through the haze, he saw a figure standing where he’d been.

Aurelion.

The transfer student shouldn’t have been able to cast without a focus. Shouldn’t have been able to summon light that color—ancient and terrible, like molten sun poured into human shape.

Aurelion raised one hand. The air warped. The void-phoenix froze mid-lunge, suspended in a prison of amber radiance.

“Be still,” Aurelion commanded.

His voice resonated with harmonics that made Kael’s teeth ache. The creature thrashed, then dissolved into harmless embers that drifted to the stone floor.

Silence swallowed the chamber.

Professor Vane stared, chalk-white. “You… you used the Old Tongue. That’s forbidden.”

Aurelion lowered his hand. His eyes—normally a soft hazel—burned with vertical pupils that contracted in the light.

“So is letting students die,” Aurelion said.

Then he grabbed Kael’s wrist.

“Run,” he hissed.

They bolted before the proctors could recover.

***

The Academy’s east wing blurred past—tapestry-lined corridors, floating candle chandeliers that flickered weakly, students gaping as they rushed by. Aurelion moved with inhuman speed, dragging Kael through service passages that smelled of dust and dying enchantments.

They burst onto the observatory balcony. The wind whipped Kael’s hair as he gasped for air. Below, the Academy’s spires stabbed into bruised purple clouds. Once, those towers had glowed with perpetual magical lamps. Now, only every third light functioned.

The Fade was accelerating.

“You’re hurting me,” Kael said.

Aurelion released his wrist. The taller boy—no, not a boy, not entirely—paced the balcony’s edge like a caged thing. His shadow moved wrong, stretching too long in the moonlight, crowned with antlers that weren’t there.

“You saw,” Aurelion said. Not a question.

“The Fade took my magic mid-cast,” Kael said. He flexed his fingers, still seeking that familiar internal warmth. Nothing. “It’s never happened that fast. Usually we get warnings. Nausea. Nosebleeds.”

“Because the Academy has been hiding the truth.” Aurelion stopped pacing. His face looked sharper now, cheekbones catching the light like blade edges. “The Ley Lines aren’t dying naturally. They’re being harvested.”

Kael laughed, bitter and short. “Harvested? By who? The weather?”

“By the Headmaster.”

The words landed like stones.

“Three hundred years ago, this school was built on a graveyard,” Aurelion continued. His voice dropped to a growl that vibrated in Kael’s chest. “Not of humans. Of my kind.”

He pulled his collar down.

Scales. Deep crimson and gold, shimmering with internal fire, tracing his collarbone and vanishing beneath his shirt.

Kael stepped back. “You’re….”

“Dragon,” Aurelion finished. “Last of the Aureate Line. And I’m done hiding.”

“You’re the key,” Kael breathed. The rumors. The prophecies whispered in the library’s restricted stacks. “The secret to restoring magic.”

Aurelion’s smile held no humor. “Yes. And tomorrow at dawn, the Headmaster intends to butcher me to fuel his ascension.”

Wind howled between the spires. Somewhere in the distance, a bell tolled eleven—three hours until the witching hour, when the Ley Lines would align.

“If he drains you, the Fade stops?” Kael asked.

“It accelerates. I’m the last anchor. When I die, the magic doesn’t return. It dies forever.” Aurelion’s eyes met his—slitted, inhuman, and terrified. “He thinks consuming me will make him a god. He’ll become a parasite in a dead world.”

Kael’s mind raced. Three years of believing the Fade was natural. A slow sunset. Now this.

“We tell the Council,” Kael said. “The Magisterium—”

“Is complicit.” Aurelion grabbed Kael’s shoulders. His fingers were hot enough to burn. “Listen to me. There’s a vault beneath the Sanctum. The original binding stones. If we destroy them, I can release the stored magic back into the Lines. But I can’t enter alone. The wards recognize my blood.”

“So you need a human.”

“I need someone who can still cast when the Fade hits.” Aurelion’s grip tightened. “Someone who’s been holding back their true nature.”

Kael’s blood ran cold. “I can’t cast. You saw—”

“You stopped casting because you were afraid of what you might become.” Aurelion touched Kael’s chest, right over his heart. “I can hear it. The drumming. The second heartbeat. You’re like me, Kael. Half-blood. The Headmaster has been grooming you as the backup vessel.”

The balcony tilted.

Memories crashed through Kael’s mind—the orphanage, the strange tests, the scholarship that had appeared exactly when his magic manifested stronger than any peer’s.

“No,” Kael whispered.

“Yes,” Aurelion said softly. “And we’re running out of time.”

***

The Sanctum’s undercroft smelled of ozone and old blood.

They’d descended through the ventilation shafts, past layers of warding that made Kael’s skin crawl. Now they stood before a door of black iron set with teeth—not carved, but *real* teeth, dragon-sized.

Aurelion pressed his palm against the metal. It hissed.

“The binding stones are inside,” he murmured. “Three of them. My mother, my sister, my brother. The Headmaster has been leeching them for centuries.”

Kael’s stomach turned. “He’s been killing your family to power the school?”

“Slowly. Carefully. Making it look like natural decline.” Aurelion’s jaw tightened. “The Fade is murder dressed up as entropy.”

Kael reached for the door handle. His hand passed through a field of static that tasted of copper.

The vault beyond was worse than he’d imagined.

Three crystal pillars dominated the circular room. Within each floated a skeleton—massive, winged, beautiful even in death. Ley Lines, visible as threads of silver light, pumped from their bones into the floor. Into the Academy. Into the Headmaster’s private chambers above.

And in the center stood the machine.

It looked like a throne crossed with a dissecting table. Manacles forged from dragon-scale steel waited open. Above them hovered a glass sphere, currently dark.

“It’s ready for me,” Aurelion said, his voice hollow.

“We destroy it,” Kael said. He raised his hands, trying to summon even a spark. Nothing came. The Fade had taken everything. “How do we destroy it?”
