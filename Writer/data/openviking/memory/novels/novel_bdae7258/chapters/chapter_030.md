Chapter 30: The Heart's Price

The astral light died between Kael’s fingertips.

Not flickered. Not faded. Died.

One moment his palm held a swirling constellation of azure fire—the next, empty air and the bitter taste of ozone. The spell’s residue burned his skin like frostbite.

"Again," whispered the voice from the tower’s shadows.

Kael gritted his teeth. Sweat traced salt lines down his neck, pooling in the hollow of his throat. The Academy's Star Tower groaned around him, ancient stones protesting the void where magic should be.

He thrust his hands toward the ceiling. "Lux eterna!"

Nothing.

Not even a spark.

"Your technique is flawless," Aurelion said.

The dragon coalesced from the darkness, scales shimmering with starlight that shouldn't exist in the dying world. His wings brushed the observatory dome, leaving trails of frost that evaporated within seconds. The creature’s golden eyes narrowed, slitted pupils contracting to dangerous pinpricks.

"The problem isn't your casting, little mortal," Aurelion rumbled. His tail swept across the dusty floor, scattering astronomical charts. "The weave itself is unraveling."

Kael lowered his trembling hands. "Then teach me the secret. You promised."

"Some secrets devour those who speak them."

Aurelion advanced, claws clicking against marble. Smoke steamed from his nostrils—silver and sweet, smelling of myrrh and lightning.

"Some require payment in souls rather than words."

Outside, the Academy bells tolled thirteen times.

An impossible hour.

Kael reached into his robes. His fingers closed around warm crystal. "I'm done waiting. The Hollow took the eastern villages last week. Instructor Marwick tried to cast a healing spell yesterday and coughed up blood."

He withdrew the relic.

The Dragon's Heart pulsed in his palm, crimson and irregular, throbbing like a true organ. Stolen from the Headmaster's vault three nights ago. It burned cold against his skin, freezing his ribs even through wool and leather.

Aurelion recoiled. His wings snapped tight against his back.

"You brought it here?" The dragon’s voice dropped to a hiss. "You absolute fool. Do you know what you've summoned?"

"The truth," Kael said. "About why you're really hiding in this tower. About why the magic is—"

The door exploded inward.

Timber fragments rained like daggers. Kael threw himself sideways, tucking the Heart against his chest. He hit the stone floor hard, shoulder screaming.

A figure stepped through the smoke.

Not Headmaster Vane. Not any professor Kael recognized, exactly.

The man wore the Academy's silver robes, but they hung wrong—too loose, as if the body inside had shrunk. His face was familiar. Second-year theoretical magics. Instructor Marwick.

But his eyes were black.

Not dark. Absent. Two holes punched through flesh into starless nothing.

"Return," Marwick said.

His voice harmonized in three octaves, the sound making Kael’s teeth ache. "The Heart belongs to the Hollow."

Aurelion lunged.

The dragon moved faster than physics allowed, becoming a streak of argent moonfire. His jaws closed around Marwick's shoulder with a sound like axes striking wood.

The instructor didn't bleed.

Shadow poured from the wound—thick, tar-black absence that smelled of ozone and endings. It splattered across the floor, eating through stone.

"Run!" Aurelion's mental voice thundered in Kael's skull, ancient and terrible. The dragon twisted, throwing Marwick against the brass telescope. Glass shattered. "The binding site! Now!"

"I can't leave you!"

"I am the bait, not the meal! Move!"

Kael ran.

Not toward the door—toward the window. Third rule of the Academy: When the interior fails, climb the exterior. He'd spent three years in these towers learning every gargoyle, every ledge, every weakness in the mortar.

He shattered the stained glass with his elbow.

Pain lanced up his arm. He didn't stop.

The Heart pulsed in his pocket, guiding him. Left. Down the gargoyle's spine. Toward the Crypt of First Arrivals.

Behind him, the tower shook. Aurelion roared—a sound that shattered windows three streets away.

Kael didn't look back.

The Crypt of First Arrivals smelled of petrichor and extinction.

Kael's boots slipped on moss-slick stones as he landed in the courtyard below. Rain hissed against the cobblestones, but the drops vanished before they hit the ground. The Hollow's influence, devouring even water.

He sprinted toward the iron doors.
