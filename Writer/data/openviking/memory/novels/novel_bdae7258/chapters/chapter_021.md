Chapter 21: The Hollow Lexicon

The vault door screamed.

Not from rust or metal fatigue—from the death of its enchantment. The azure runes etched into the ancient steel flickered like candles in a hurricane, spitting sparks of dying mana into the dusty air.

Kael slammed his shoulder against the barrier. Again. The impact rattled his bones.

"Left hinge!" Lyra shouted over the groaning metal. She pressed her thaumaturgical lens against the seam, her eyes narrowing at the readout. "The preservation weave is unraveling three times faster than predicted. We have maybe four minutes before total structural collapse."

"Working on it," Kael grunted.

He raised his palm, willing the familiar heat of fire magic to manifest. Nothing happened. Just a hollow coldness where his power used to live, like blowing into an empty bottle.

"Don't," Lyra warned, grabbing his wrist. Her fingers were ice-cold. "You'll accelerate the decay. Use the crowbar."

Kael swore and grabbed the iron bar from his pack. This was the new reality of the Dying Magic. Three months ago, he could have melted the door with a thought. Now he was reduced to brute force and primitive tools.

He wedged the crowbar into the gap and heaved.

The vault shuddered. Somewhere deep inside, something ancient and magical shattered. The runes flared blinding white, then died forever.

The door swung open.

***

The Etheric Archive of Vel Morath had stood for nine centuries. Now it was committing suicide.

Kael stepped through the threshold into a cathedral of decay. Floating shelves drifted aimlessly, their levitation spells sputtering. Books tumbled through the air like wounded birds, their pages turning to ash before hitting the marble floor.

"It's worse than the eastern provinces," Lyra whispered, her voice tight with scientific horror. She clutched her analyzer, the brass device clicking frantically. "The background thaumaturgic density is nearly zero. We're standing in a null-magic zone."

"Then where's the Lexicon?" Kael asked, scanning the chaos.

"Center aisle. If the preservation charms held—"

"They didn't."

A bookshelf to their right dissolved. Not fell apart. Dissolved. The wood aged centuries in seconds, crumbling to dust that smelled of forgotten summers and dead gods.

Kael sprinted down the aisle, boots crunching on the remains of enchanted tapestries. The Prime Lexicon—the last complete record of pre-fading magical theory—sat on a crystal pedestal ahead. But even from here, he could see the damage.

The book was becoming translucent.

"Kael, hurry!" Lyra called. She was struggling with her equipment, trying to record the phenomenon even as her instruments failed. "It's phasing out of reality!"

He reached the pedestal as the Lexicon's edges began to blur. His hands passed through the cover once, twice—ghost fingers grasping at vanishing knowledge.

Third time, he caught solid leather.

"I've got it!"

The moment his skin touched the ancient binding, pain lanced up his arm. Not physical—existential. A vision slammed into his mind: darkness consuming stars, magic being drawn into a void that hungered, always hungry, wearing a familiar face—

"Kael!"

Lyra's voice snapped him back. He clutched the Lexicon to his chest and stumbled away from the pedestal. The crystal stand crackled and turned to mundane glass, then sand.

"We need to move," Kael gasped, sweat pouring down his face. "This whole place is going to—"

"Going to what, Vane?" The voice cut through the chaos like a scalpel. "Crumble? Collapse? Return to the natural state of things?"

Malachar stood at the archive entrance.

He hadn't been there five seconds ago. Now the tall, robed figure filled the doorway, his pristine white garments contrasting sharply with the decay around him. In his hand, he held a staff that pulsed with unnatural darkness—a darkness that seemed to drink the light rather than block it.

"You," Kael growled, shifting into a combat stance despite the emptiness in his magical reserves.

"Me," Malachar agreed, stepping inside. The falling dust avoided him, creating a perfect sphere of cleanliness. "Though I must say, you're working terribly hard to preserve what is already dead. The Lexicon belongs in a museum, Kael. Or better yet, a pyre."

Lyra moved to Kael's side, her hand drifting toward the chemical grenades at her belt. "You're accelerating the fade. Your experiments in the northern wastes—we've seen the readings. You're not studying the death of magic. You're causing it."

"Causing?" Malachar laughed, the sound like breaking glass. "No, my dear. I'm *harvesting* it. There's a difference."

He raised his staff. The darkness pulsed.

Around them, the decay accelerated. Books burst into dust. Stone pillars cracked as their reinforcement spells vanished. The air itself felt thinner, as if the world was losing the will to exist.

"Give me the Lexicon," Malachar commanded. "It contains the location of the First Spring. The source of all magic. If I drain it, the transition will be painless. Order will replace chaos. A world of logic, not superstition."

"Over my dead body," Kael said.

"That can be arranged."

Malachar struck the floor with his staff.

The shockwave knocked Kael off his feet. He crashed into a collapsing shelf, the Lexicon tumbling from his grasp. Pain exploded in his ribs. When he looked up, Malachar was advancing, each step leaving footprints of absolute blackness on the marble.

"You're pathetic," Malachar sneered. "Clinging to your spells like a child to a blanket. Can't you feel it, Kael? The emptiness? Magic is a crutch, and I am curing the world of its—"

The ceiling exploded.

Stone and timber rained down, but stopped ten feet above the floor, suspended in a field of golden light. A shape descended through the hole—massive, scaled, wings that beat against the dying air with the force of a hurricane.

Aurelion had arrived.

The dragon landed between Kael and Malachar, his ancient body filling the aisle. His scales, usually the color of burnished gold, were visibly dimmed, patchy with grey. Even the great wyrm was not immune to the fading.

But his eyes still burned with starlight.

"Malachar," Aurelion rumbled, his voice resonating in Kael's chest. "You trespass in the house of memory."

"Aurelion," Malachar said, his confidence faltering for the first time. "I thought you were hibernating. Dying in your sleep like the rest of your obsolete kind."

"Not yet, usurper."

The dragon breathed.

Not fire—something older. Primal essence that existed before magic had rules or names. The golden light washed over Malachar, who shrieked and raised his staff. Darkness met light in a screaming collision that shattered what remained of the archive windows.

Kael scrambled for the Lexicon. His fingers closed on the spine just as Malachar broke free of Aurelion's breath, his white robes smoking.

"This isn't over!" Malachar shrieked, backing toward the door. "The Spring is mine, dragon! I've already found the map!"

He vanished in a swirl of shadows—a teleportation spell that cost more magic than most kingdoms possessed in a year. The extravagance of it, even now, made Kael sick.

Aurelion slumped, his wings drooping. "He grows stronger as we weaken."

Lyra rushed to Kael, helping him stand. "Are you alright? Your nose is bleeding."

Kael wiped his face, smearing blood across his sleeve. "Fine. Aurelion, he said he has the map to the First Spring. If he drains it—"

"Then magic dies not with a whimper, but with his coronation as god of a mundane world," Aurelion finished. The dragon lowered his great head until his snout was level with Kael's chest. His eyes, ancient and sad, fixed on the Lexicon. "Open it."

"What?"

"The book. Now. While it still exists."

Kael fumbled with the clasp. The Lexicon felt lighter than it should, as if half its pages had already faded into myth. He opened it to a random spread.

The text was glowing. Not the soft phosphorescence of preservation magic, but urgent, desperate light.

"It's... it's writing itself," Lyra breathed, leaning over his shoulder. "No, wait. It's translating. Adapting to the reader."

The glowing words resolved into Common tongue:

*The vessel approaches. The sinkhole walks on two legs. Magic does not die; it is devoured. The devourer knows not his hunger.*

Kael's blood ran cold. "What does that mean?"

Aurelion's gaze met his, heavy with terrible knowledge. "Read the last line, Kael. Read who the vessel is."

Lyra turned the page. The light grew blinding, searing the words into Kael's retinas:

*Kael Vane. Born of the Spring. The Hollow King.*

"What?" Kael staggered back. "No. That's impossible. I'm trying to save magic, not—"

"Every spell you've cast these three months," Aurelion said softly. "Every time you reached for fire and found emptiness. You weren't failing to draw magic, young one. You were consuming it. The power isn't fading from the world. It's funneling into you."

The Lexicon dropped from Kael's numb fingers. It hit the ground and shattered like porcelain, its purpose served, its knowledge delivered.

"I can feel it now," Aurelion whispered. "The hunger in you. Growing. If you cast one more spell, Kael—if you draw upon the Spring even once more—you will devour the last ember of magic in this world. And you will become exactly what Malachar wants: the final grave of wonder."

Kael looked at his hands. The hollow feeling wasn't emptiness.

It was appetite.

And it was waking up.
