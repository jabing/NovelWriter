Chapter 17: The Seventh Weave

The spell shattered at the seventh weave.

Kael felt the mana unravel like wet silk through his fingers, slippery and treacherous. One moment, the Luminous Arc hung suspended above the practice circle—a perfect lattice of silver light that should have hardened into a shield capable of turning aside crossbow bolts. The next, it collapsed into stardust that never touched the floor.

It simply ceased to be.

"Again," Kael whispered. His voice cracked in the empty chamber, echoing off the vaulted ceiling where painted dragons battled in frescoes that were themselves beginning to fade.

He raised his hands, tracing the familiar patterns. First the anchor, knotted tight in his chest. Then the weave, fingers dancing through the proper geometries. Finally the binding, the command that should have locked the structure into reality. His fingers left trails of emerald light that should have solidified into a protective sphere. Instead, they flickered. Faded. Died.

Three weeks ago, this spell had been effortless.

Kael stared at his palms. The lines there itched, as if the magic wanted to come but couldn't remember how. He reached for his reserves—that warm pool of power every student learned to sense during their first year—and found it diminished. Not just depleted from casting. Gone. Like dipping a cup into a well and hearing the spade scrape stone.

Outside the practice chamber's grand windows, the truth no one wanted to name grew more obvious by the hour. The Sky-Trees of Valendria were turning gray. Their bioluminescent leaves, once bright enough to read by at midnight, looked like ash against the twilight sky. Even now, one of the ancient branches cracked, falling three hundred feet to shatter in the courtyard below. No magic cushioned its landing.

The magic was running out.

"Stop, Mr. Thorne." The voice came from the observation balcony, sharp as broken glass.

Kael spun, heart hammering. Professor Elara stood silhouetted against the stained glass, her robes the color of dried blood. She held a measuring crystal that pulsed with sickly yellow light instead of healthy azure. In her other hand, she gripped the railing so tightly her knuckles matched her white hair.

"You're wasting your reserves," she said, descending the spiral stairs. Her boots clicked against stone with a sound like cracking ice. "The ambient mana in this room dropped forty percent while you were casting."

"I can fix it," Kael said. Sweat trickled down his spine. "I just need to adjust the harmonic resonance. Maybe if I—"

"You can't adjust extinction, boy." Elara thrust the crystal toward him. Inside, the magical resonance looked like a heartbeat monitor flatlining. Each pulse was weaker than the last, the gaps between them stretching longer and longer. "Look at it. The weave isn't failing because you're clumsy. It's failing because there's nothing left to weave with."

Kael took the crystal. It felt cold. Wrong. Three months ago, holding such a device would have made his skin tingle. Now it felt like holding deadwood.

"The Board meets tonight," Elara said, her voice dropping to a whisper. She glanced toward the doors as if afraid of listeners. "They're voting to seal the lower archives. Burn the dangerous texts before the enchantments holding them fail completely."

Kael's chest tightened. "The Dragon Codex is down there."

"Exactly."

Elara's eyes—normally sharp as scalpels, capable of dissecting a student's magical flaws from twenty paces—held something dangerous. Pity? Fear? Or was that desperation?

"Aurelion isn't a myth," she said softly. "And if the Codex burns, we lose our only map to finding him before the last lights go out. We lose the secret."

"You know where he is?" Kael stepped closer. "Professor, if the dragon really holds the key to restoring magic—"

"I know that the Board thinks he's a battery to be drained." Elara pressed something into Kael's palm. A brass key, ice-cold, etched with symbols that hurt to look at directly. "Third sub-basement. Left past the statues that weep mercury. You have until moonrise before the wards fail and the fire-purges begin."

Kael stared at the key. It weighed more than metal should. It tasted of old magic—the deep kind that predated the Academy's founding stones, the kind that supposedly never faded because it was part of the world's bones.

"Why me?" he asked.

"Because your spell residue reads like dragon-fire," Elara said, turning away. Her shoulders slumped. "And because you're the only student reckless enough to try what needs to be done. Don't make me regret this, Thorne."

The door to the practice chamber clicked shut with a sound like a coffin closing.

Kael stood alone in the failing light.

He moved.
