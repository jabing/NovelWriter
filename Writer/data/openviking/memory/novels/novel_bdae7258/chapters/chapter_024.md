**Chapter 24: The Price of Light**

The crystal shattered.

Not exploded—shattered. Like ice under a hammer, the containment gem fractured into gray dust that never touched the ground. It simply... stopped existing.

Kael Vane stared at his outstretched hand. The arcane light that should have danced between his fingers had vanished. In its place, a sickly yellow pallor clung to his skin, the color of dying leaves.

"Failure." Archivist Theron's voice cut through the stunned silence of the examination hall. "Again."

Whispers erupted among the thirty other students. Kael lowered his hand, his pulse hammering against his ribs. Three weeks ago, that same binding spell had contained a storm elemental. Today, it couldn't hold a candle flame.

"It's not him," Lyra Ashford said. She pushed through the crowd, her dark hair escaping its braid. "The ley-lines in this sector are destabilized. Look at the residue."

She pointed to the dust. Instead of the usual azure sparkle of spent mana, the particles were turning black, crumbling into nothing.

Theron adjusted his spectacles. His face had gone pale. "The Fading accelerates."

The words hung in the air like a death sentence.

In the corner of the hall, a senior student attempted a simple illumination cantrip. The light flickered twice, then died. Someone sobbed.

Kael met Lyra's eyes. The fear there mirrored his own. They'd studied the theoretical limits of magical entropy. They'd calculated the decline in arcane density across the continent. But theory was different from watching reality unravel.

"We need to see Aurelion," Kael said quietly.

Lyra nodded. "Now. Before the Academy locks down."

The tunnel beneath the East Wing had existed for four centuries, but the stone had never felt so cold. Kael pressed his palm against the recognition sigil. The familiar tingle of verification magic felt weak, like a static shock rather than the usual warm embrace.

The wall rumbled aside.

"You're late," Aurelion said.

The dragon filled the cavern. Not with his body—though the ancient wyrm coiled across the chamber like a mountain of molten gold—but with his presence. Pressure against the mind. Weight against the soul.

His scales, usually brilliant as captured sunlight, had dulled to bronze. The bioluminescent veins tracing his wings pulsed irregularly, arrhythmic heartbeats of a dying star. Where his tail touched the floor, the stone had begun to crumble into sand that glittered with dying potential.

Kael bowed. "The Fading—"

"I felt it." Aurelion's voice was earthquakes and distant thunder. "Felt the threads snapping. Three more ley-nodes collapsed in the Western Reaches while you took your test. The Healer's Guild in Valis has gone dark. No more miracles. Only medicine."

Lyra stepped forward, clutching her grimoire. "The mathematical models suggest we have months. Maybe a year. But today's collapse rate indicates—"

"Days." Aurelion lowered his massive head. One amber eye, slit-pupiled and ancient, focused on Kael. "Perhaps hours. The Dying is no longer gradual, young Vane. It has become an avalanche."

Kael's mouth went dry. "You said you knew why. You said you held the key."

"I do." Smoke curled from Aurelion's nostrils, gray instead of gold. It smelled of ozone and burning libraries. "And I have withheld it. For good reason."

"Withheld?" Kael took a step forward. "People are losing their magic! The healers at St. Alms can't close wounds. The containment wards around the city—"

"Are failing. I know." Aurelion's tail scraped against stone, sending sparks that died before they touched the ground. "The key requires a price, Kael. One I hoped never to demand. One I prayed would not be necessary in my lifetime."

Lyra's hand found Kael's arm. Her fingers trembled against his sleeve. "What price?"

Aurelion was silent for a long moment. The bioluminescent veins in his wings dimmed further. When he spoke, the cavern seemed to shrink. "The Fading is not natural. It is consumption. Something devours magic itself, from the root. To stop it, I must give what remains of my essence to refill the well."

"You'll die," Kael whispered.
