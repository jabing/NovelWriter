The containment sphere shattered at exactly midnight.

Kael dove left as jagged shards of crystalized mana sprayed across the laboratory. Each fragment struck the stone floor with a sound like breaking bones, hissing as the last vestiges of enchantment evaporated into gray smoke.

"Stabilize the northern anchor!" he shouted.

Mira was already moving, her hands weaving patterns that should have glowed amber. Instead, pale sparks sputtered from her fingertips like dying fireflies. The air smelled of ozone and rust—the scent of magic rotting from the inside out.

"It’s not responding," Mira gasped. Her braids whipped around her face as arcane wind tore through the chamber. "Kael, the leyline—it’s gone completely dead."

The experiment had been simple enough twelve hours ago: preserve a basic light enchantment using theoretical stabilization techniques. Now the spell-cascade was consuming itself, devouring ambient magic faster than the dying world could supply it.

Kael slammed his palm against the emergency dispersal rune. The stone remained cold and dark.

"Of course," he muttered. "Even the failsafes are failing."

He reached into his coat pocket, fingers closing around warm scales. Aurelion stirred, the dragon’s consciousness brushing against his mind like silk dragged over broken glass.

*Let me,* the dragon whispered.

"No," Kael said aloud. "Not here. They’ll sense you."

But the darkness was spreading. The unraveling spell had begun to warp reality itself. Kael watched in horror as a stack of reference tomes crumbled—not burning, not tearing, but simply ceasing to exist, their magical composition unwriting itself from existence.

"We have to evacuate the east wing," Mira said, coughing through the thickening haze. "If this reaches the artifact vaults—"

A boom rocked the Academy foundations.

The laboratory doors blew inward, splinters raining across the workbenches. Professor Vane stood silhouetted against the corridor’s guttering torchlight, his robes snapping in the magical backdraft. In his right hand, he carried a manacle forged of black iron and absence itself.

"Step away from the cascade, Initiate Thorne," Vane commanded. His voice carried the weight of old authority, the kind that had already decided outcomes. "And bring me the dragon."

Kael moved without thinking, putting his body between the professor and the pocket where Aurelion hid. The dragon’s heartbeat hammered against his ribs, rapid and ancient.

"I don’t know what you’re—"

"Don’t insult me, boy." Vane advanced, each footstep leaving frost that didn’t melt. "The creature’s aura has been bleeding through your wards for weeks. Did you think the Council wouldn’t notice a font of pure magic walking through our halls disguised as a student’s pet?"

The dying spell-cascade between them pulsed, a wound in the air. Through it, Kael could see the truth of Vane’s words—the Professor’s eyes reflected no light. They were voids, hungry voids, drinking in the dissipating enchantments.

"You’re harvesting it," Kael realized. "The dying magic. You’re not trying to save it."

Vane smiled. It was worse than his frown.

"Magic isn’t dying, child. It’s being collected. Refined." He raised the manacle. "And Aurelion is the last reservoir. The battery that will power the new age—once properly drained."

Mira’s hand found Kael’s shoulder. Her grip trembled, but her voice stayed steady. "There are two of us, Professor. Third-year combat protocols. You’re old, and that toy looks heavy."

"Foolish girl."

Vane snapped his fingers.

The floor beneath Mira turned to liquid shadow. She sank to her knees, screaming as dark tendrils wrapped her limbs. Kael lunged for her, but an invisible force seized his throat, lifting him until his boots dangled above the stone.

"Your generation doesn’t understand sacrifice," Vane said, circling them like a shark in deep water. "When the wells run dry, you dig deeper. You don’t weep over the picks you break."

Kael choked, clawing at the force constricting his windpipe. Black spots danced in his vision. Through the haze, he felt Aurelion uncoiling, the dragon’s protective rage building like a storm behind his sternum.

*Release me,* Aurelion commanded. *Or we both end here.*

Kael had no choice. He opened the pocket.

Light exploded—not the warm gold of hearth fires or the sharp silver of moonlight, but something older. Something that remembered when the world was young and words had power because they were spoken for the first time.

Aurelion emerged, no larger than a hound but burning with the density of collapsed stars. His scales shifted through spectrums human eyes weren’t meant to perceive, and his wings unfolded like theorems written in fire.

Vane stumbled back, shielding his face. "Beautiful," he breathed. "Perfect."

The shadow restraints holding Mira dissolved. She collapsed, gasping, but Kael remained suspended, the Professor’s will focused entirely on maintaining the chokehold.

"You see?" Vane extended the manacle. "Even now, it wastes power protecting you. Sentiment is a luxury, dragon. A waste of potential."

Aurelion’s voice resonated in the space between heartbeats. *You mistake protection for purpose, mortal. I do not guard this boy. I choose him.*

"Then choose oblivion."

Vane struck.

The manacle sailed through the air, expanding, its iron mouth opening to swallow light itself. Aurelion met it with a blade of concentrated essence—claw against anti-magic forged in the deep places where magic went to die.

The collision shattered every window in the east wing.

Kael hit the floor, the telekinetic grip broken. He rolled, tasting blood, and scrambled toward Mira. She was conscious, her hands already sketching emergency wards, but her eyes were fixed on the combat overhead.

Aurelion and Vane clashed in the center of the room, reality buckling around them. Where the dragon passed, color returned—fleeting, fragile, but real. Where Vane struck, the world grayed, textures smoothing into featureless dullness.

"He’s eating it," Mira whispered. "Kael, he’s eating the magic Aurelion produces."

She was right. Each time Aurelon healed a fracture in the air, Vane absorbed the excess, growing younger, stronger, more terrible. The Professor had become a singularity, a walking famine.

"We need to break the circle," Kael said, dragging Mira toward the laboratory’s secondary exit. "The cascade—it’s feeding him too. It’s a trap."

But Aurelion was slowing. The dragon’s light dimmed with each exchange, and Kael felt the corresponding drain in his own soul—the bond between them translating pain across species.

*The secret,* Aurelion’s voice came weakly in Kael’s mind. *You must know now. Before he takes it.*

"Tell me!"

*To restore the flow, there must be a bridge. A channel willing to burn.*

Vane landed a blow. Aurelion crashed into the shattered remains of the containment sphere, scales cracking like porcelain. The dragon’s blood hit the floor—not red, but liquid starlight, and where it splashed, wild magic erupted in uncontrolled growth.

Crystal formations sprouted from the stone. Gravity twisted. Kael felt his memories trying to detach, floating just out of reach.

"The bridge," Kael repeated, crawling toward the dragon through the chaos. "What does that mean?"

Aurelion’s eyes—ancient, kind, impossibly tired—met his.

*It means I cannot save your world alone. I can only open the door. Someone must walk through. Someone must become the new source.*

"Become?" Kael’s voice cracked. "Aurelion, what happens to the bridge?"

The dragon didn’t answer. Instead, he began to sing.

It was not sound, but structure. The song rewrote the parameters of the room, converting stone to potential, air to promise. The cascade that had been destroying magic reversed, becoming a vortex that pulled inward, toward Aurelion’s wounded heart.

Vane screamed in fury and fear. "No! You’ll destroy it! You’ll destroy everything!"

"He’s not destroying anything," Mira said, understanding dawning in her voice. "He’s transferring it."

Kael felt the pull. The song was calling him, recognizing the resonance of their bond. He understood now, with perfect clarity, why Aurelion had hidden in his pocket, why the dragon had lingered at the Academy of dying arts.

The dragon hadn’t been waiting to be found.

He’d been waiting to choose a successor.

"Kael," Mira grabbed his arm, her fingers digging in. "Don’t. Look at him. Look what it costs."

Aurelion was dissolving. Not dying—transcending—but the process was erasing his individuality, his memories, his name. He was becoming pure conduit, and he was offering Kael the other end of that conduit.

If Kael accepted, he would not die.

He would become the well from which all magic flowed.

Forever. Alone. Burning.

Vane lunged, manacle raised, desperate to interrupt the transfer. "I won’t let you! The power is mine! I earned it!"

Kael looked at the manacle, then at Aurelion, then at Mira’s terrified face.

He made his choice.

The laboratory ceiling exploded as the Academy’s wards finally failed, revealing not sky, but the raw, hungry void that waited beyond the dying world. And in that terrible silence, Kael took the dragon’s offered claw, and the burning began.
