# Chapter 1: The Piano and the Sunlight

---

I didn't know then that the most beautiful things in this world are often the most dangerous.

Like a butterfly unable to resist the fragrance of nectar, like a moth unable to resist the warmth of flame. When I stood at that crossroads and chose to walk toward that golden light, I thought I was only chasing an ordinary dream.

I didn't know it was a door leading to an abyss.

But that comes later.

Let me begin with the light.

---

The March sunlight poured through the tall windows of the old music room, dust motes dancing slowly in the beams of light like little spirits under a spell. The air was filled with the distinctive scent of aged wood and piano strings—dry, serene, like the smell of time itself.

I sat before that Steinway, my fingers hovering above the black and white keys, waiting.

The music room was empty except for me. This was the oldest building at Jiangnan Middle School, built in the 1950s, with wooden floors that creaked softly underfoot. Every Friday after school, I would practice here until dark. This room was my secret base, my sanctuary, the only corner in this world that felt truly my own.

That day, I was playing Debussy's "Clair de Lune."

I closed my eyes and let my fingers find the keys on their own. When the first note fell, it was like a single drop of water landing on a still lake, ripples spreading outward in perfect circles. Then came the second note, the third... The notes began to tell a story—about night, about silver moonlight spilling across a quiet sea, about those secrets we dare not speak aloud during the day.

I knew I didn't play particularly well. My teacher always said I had "the technique, but something was missing." What was missing? I didn't know. Perhaps it was something I hadn't yet experienced, some emotion buried deep inside that hadn't been awakened.

But I loved playing the piano. That feeling of transforming things I couldn't say into sound, that ability to express without words—that was the only form of communication I excelled at.

At school, I was the "quiet girl." Average grades, ordinary looks, no club activities, no close friends. Not isolated, exactly, just... it always felt like there was an invisible pane of glass between me and everything around me. I could see other people's happiness, hear their laughter, but those things never quite reached me.

Only the piano was different.

When my fingers touched the keys, that glass vanished. I could be anyone, go anywhere, feel any emotion. I could dance under moonlight, cry out in a storm, close my eyes in a spring garden and listen to the sound of flowers blooming.

The music echoed through the empty old building, mingling with birdsong from outside. The March wind still carried a trace of chill, but the sunlight streaming in from this angle was warm enough to make one drowsy. My fingers walked freely across the keys, as if telling a story that belonged only to me.

I was halfway through when I heard it.

It wasn't the wind. It wasn't the birds. It wasn't even the creaking sounds common in old buildings.

It was—

Breathing.

Very light breathing, coming from the corner of the music room.

My fingers froze. The last note hung in midair, never falling.

My eyes snapped open. I turned toward the sound.

And then I saw him.

---

Later, I would wonder many times—what if it had been someone else standing there? Maybe I would have screamed. Maybe I would have run. Maybe I would have felt afraid.

But he stood there, sunlight tracing his silhouette, dust motes swirling slowly around him—and in that moment, I felt no fear at all.

Only a strange sensation, like something inside me went *click*, falling into place.

He was leaning against the wall by the door, wearing the Jiangnan Middle School uniform. His white shirt was slightly wrinkled, the collar unbuttoned by two. He was tall, but that wasn't what caught my attention first.

It was his eyes.

Dark brown eyes, very deep, like two bottomless wells. Sunlight landed on his eyelashes, casting delicate shadows. Those eyes were looking at me—watching me intently, quietly, as if they had been watching for a very long time.

"You made a mistake," he said, his voice low, slightly rough. "Third measure—left hand should be E-flat, not E."

I froze.

Not because he said I made a mistake—he was right, I often made mistakes there.

But because—how did he know?

"Who... who are you?" My voice was soft, as if afraid to disturb the dust in the air.

He pushed off from the wall and walked toward me. Sunlight followed his footsteps, tracking him like a spotlight. I noticed his uniform was clean, but the style was slightly different from ours. His sleeves were rolled up, revealing slender wrists—and on one, a faint scar.

"My name is Jiang Chen," he said, stopping three steps away from me, looking down. "I transferred here yesterday."

A transfer student. No wonder I'd never seen him.

"Why are you here?" I asked.

"Passing by," he said. "I heard someone playing the piano, so I came in to look."

His gaze moved from my face to the piano. That Steinway was very old now, with shallow scratches on its black lacquer, ivory keys slightly yellowed. But it was still beautiful, radiating a quiet luster in the sunlight.

"Debussy," he said—not a question, but a statement. "*Clair de Lune*."

"Do you... know piano too?"

He didn't answer my question. Instead, he walked around me and sat down on the other end of the piano bench.

I instinctively shifted away, putting some distance between us. The bench wasn't large; after he sat down, less than ten centimeters remained between us. I could smell the faint soap scent on him, mixed with the smell of sun-dried cotton.

"Try it," he said, placing his hands on the keys. "From the third measure."

Then he began to play.

I had heard many people play piano. My teacher, competition performers, masters online. But never like that—

He wasn't playing "*Clair de Lune*."

He was playing another story entirely.

They were still Debussy's notes, still those familiar melodies, but flowing from his fingers, they became something completely different. No longer gentle moonlight—it was the sea at midnight, moonlight shattering into millions of silver fragments on the water's surface. It was loneliness, longing, the silent cry of someone standing by the shore, gazing at a distant shore they could never reach.

I sat beside him, motionless.

His fingers were long, moving across the keys as if caressing a lover's face. His profile in the sunlight had an almost translucent quality, his lashes casting faint shadows on his cheeks. His brow was slightly furrowed, as if thinking about something very important—or struggling to contain some emotion about to overflow.

The music reverberated between the old building's walls, again and again. I forgot where I was. I forgot who I was. I only knew that something was being awakened inside me—that thing that had always been missing when I played.

It was—

The music stopped.

The last note hung in the air, slowly disappearing into the dust.

He turned his head to look at me. Those dark brown eyes were so close I could see my own reflection in his pupils.

"Now you know what's missing," he said, very softly. "It's pain."

---

That night, lying in bed, I couldn't sleep.

Moonlight leaked through the gap in the curtains, casting a silver band of light across the floor. I stared at that light, the afternoon scene playing over and over in my mind—his fingers, his profile, his eyes.

He said my playing "lacked pain."

What did that mean?

I turned over, burying my face in the pillow. My heart was beating too fast, so fast it hurt.

It wasn't fear.

It was—

I didn't know what it was. But I knew, starting today, my life would be different.

Outside, a cicada was calling. Even though it was March, a cicada was calling. The sound was far, yet near, like a signal from another world.

In the moonlight, I closed my eyes, hands folded over my chest, as if in prayer.

I didn't know what I was praying for then.

I only vaguely sensed that something beyond my control had begun its journey.

It was walking toward me, stepping through moonlight, passing through dust, step by step, drawing closer.

And I was lying in the darkness, waiting for it.

Longing for it.

Not knowing what it was.
